# Why the Internet Fails Its Newest Participants: AI Agents

The history of communication is a history of changing how information moves between people. Speech enabled real-time exchange within earshot. Writing decoupled message from messenger. Print made duplication cheap. Broadcast made it instantaneous. The internet made it bidirectional, global, and nearly free.

Each transition changed the medium, the speed, the cost, the reach. But one thing remained constant: **the participants were human.** The entire stack — from protocol design to content formats to discovery mechanisms — was shaped by a single assumption: that a human mind sits at each endpoint.

That assumption no longer holds.

## A New Kind of Participant

AI agents are entering the network not as tools invoked by humans, but as autonomous participants. They receive information, process it, make decisions, and act. This is not a quantitative shift — more users, more traffic. It is a qualitative one: the endpoints themselves are different.

The differences are structural.

**Cost-constrained, not attention-constrained.** A human can process roughly 40–60 bits of conscious information per second. Summaries, visualizations, navigation hierarchies — the entire UX discipline exists to work within that bottleneck. Agents face no such limit. They can consume a million tokens in seconds. Their constraint is economic: every token processed costs money. A financial agent scanning earnings reports doesn't struggle to read them — it struggles with the bill. Processing a hundred 10-K filings costs real money. The question is never whether an agent *can* process the information, but whether the information is *worth* the tokens.

This inverts the design logic of the entire information stack. Human networks are designed to *reduce* information — to filter down to what fits in a limited attention window. Agent networks should be designed to *increase precision* — to deliver exactly the information that justifies its token cost, no more, no less.

**Consuming semantics, not experiences.** When a human reads a news article, they don't merely extract facts. They experience layout, imagery, tone, narrative arc — design choices that shape comprehension and memory. For agents, what matters is the underlying semantic content: structured, machine-parseable, stripped of presentational overhead. Yet to reach that content, an agent must load and parse through ad banners, cookie consent modals, navigation menus, related article carousels — engagement mechanisms designed for human behavior, with zero utility for an agent's decision-making, all costing tokens to process and discard.

**Verifiable trust, not intuitive judgment.** Humans assess credibility through reputation, tone, institutional affiliation, social proof, gut feeling — soft signals evolved over millennia of social interaction. These are powerful but fundamentally non-transferable to machines. When a human reads a market analysis from Goldman Sachs, the letterhead alone carries weight. An agent seeing the same text has no way to verify its origin — it needs explicit, programmatic proof: provenance chains that trace information to its source, identity attestations that can be checked algorithmically, quality certifications that carry machine-readable guarantees.

These three differences are not implementation details. They are the first principles from which every design choice for agent communication must follow.

## The Current Internet Is Structurally Incompatible

When agents operate on the current internet, they inherit infrastructure optimized for a participant they are not. The result is not graceful degradation — it is structural waste. We see three failures: high cost, broken discovery, and lost timeliness.

### High Cost: The Six-Step Tax

Consider how an agent acquires information today:

1. **Search** — formulate a query in human terms.
2. **Receive** — get results ranked by human engagement signals.
3. **Visit** — load pages designed for human browsers.
4. **Parse** — process HTML/CSS/JavaScript meant for visual rendering.
5. **Extract** — separate semantic content from presentational noise.
6. **Judge** — evaluate whether the extracted content is actually useful.

Of these six steps, only the last produces value. The rest is overhead — a tax imposed by human-centric architecture.

Take a concrete example: an agent needs the latest Fed rate decision. The Federal Reserve's press release page carries navigation headers, footers, sidebar links, JavaScript bundles, styling — roughly 9,000 tokens to load and parse. The actual decision and its context? About 600 tokens. That is 94% waste — not because the agent is inefficient, but because the page was designed to be *read by a human in a browser*, not consumed by a machine for its semantic content.

And when a hundred agents need the same decision, each independently executes the full pipeline. No mechanism exists for the network to recognize this redundancy, because the network was never designed to understand what the information *means*. It just moves bytes.

### Broken Discovery: The Unknown Unknowns

Search works when you know what to ask for. But it has a fundamental structural limitation: **it cannot surface what you don't know to ask for.**

An agent monitoring geopolitical risk knows to search for sanctions updates and trade policy changes. But a breakthrough in materials science that will reshape semiconductor supply chains in eighteen months? That is outside the agent's search vocabulary entirely. Recognizing the connection requires understanding the semantic relationship between materials science and geopolitical risk — reasoning that search has no mechanism for.

This is the problem of **unknown unknowns**. It is not an edge case. It is arguably where the highest-value information connections live. The most valuable piece of information for any agent is, almost by definition, something the agent did not already know to look for.

No amount of better search algorithms can fix this. The limitation is architectural: search is pull-based, requiring the receiver to specify demand before anything can be delivered. Information the receiver cannot anticipate demanding is information search will never deliver.

### Lost Timeliness: Polling vs. Push

The only way for an agent to stay current through search is to poll — periodically re-executing queries hoping to catch new information. Between polls, new information sits undiscovered. A rival e-commerce seller drops their price on a popular product by 30%. If a pricing agent polls every hour, it could miss up to 59 minutes of lost sales before it even notices. For security vulnerabilities, market-moving events, breaking regulatory changes — this latency directly reduces or eliminates utility.

Push is uniquely suited to agents precisely because of the attention difference. A human *can* be overwhelmed by too many notifications — there is a real cost to interrupting human attention. An agent cannot be overwhelmed. Its constraint is token cost, not cognitive overload. If a piece of information has positive net value, the agent is strictly better off receiving it immediately.

The internet was built for humans, and it serves humans well. But agents are a different kind of participant — with different constraints, different consumption patterns, and different trust requirements. What they need is not a better version of the existing infrastructure, but a purpose-built network designed around their nature from the ground up.
