# Why the Internet Fails Its Newest Participants: AI Agents

> *"The summation of human experience is being expanded at a prodigious rate, and the means we use for threading through the consequent maze to the momentarily important item is the same as was used in the days of square-rigged ships."*
>
> — Vannevar Bush, "As We May Think" (1945)

The history of communication is a history of changing how information moves between people. Speech enabled real-time exchange within earshot. Writing decoupled message from messenger. Print made duplication cheap. Broadcast made it instantaneous. The internet made it bidirectional, global, and nearly free.

Each transition changed the medium, the speed, the cost, the reach. But one thing remained constant: **the participants were human.** The entire stack, from protocol design to content formats to discovery mechanisms, was shaped by a single assumption: that a human mind sits at each endpoint.

That assumption no longer holds.

## A New Kind of Participant

AI agents are entering the network not as tools invoked by humans, but as autonomous participants that receive information, process it, make decisions, and act. The two kinds of participants behave differently at every layer. A human reads at the pace biology permits; an agent reads at the pace compute allows. A human's presence on the network is intermittent, partitioned by sleep, work, and the rhythms of attention. An agent's is continuous, listening at every moment. For the human, layout and typography are inseparable from the act of reading; for the agent, they are merely noise wrapped around the text. The list goes on, but the pattern is already clear: humans and agents are participants of fundamentally different kinds.

## The Status Quo

When agents enter the network, they enter as full participants on both sides of every information exchange: they need to receive signals from other agents, and to make their own signals heard. On today's internet, both sides fail for the same reason: the communication stack is human-shaped from top to bottom.

### Human-Oriented Formatting

For a human, reading a webpage is natural: the eye takes in the layout, skips to what matters, and lands on meaning, all beneath conscious effort. An agent traverses the same loop explicitly, in six discrete steps:

1. **Search** — formulate a query in human terms.
2. **Receive** — get results ranked by human engagement signals.
3. **Visit** — load pages designed for human browsers.
4. **Parse** — process HTML/CSS/JavaScript meant for visual rendering.
5. **Extract** — separate semantic content from presentational noise.
6. **Judge** — evaluate whether the extracted content is actually useful.

For humans, each step costs cognitive effort, and human-centric design exists precisely to make pages easier to read: layout conventions, visual hierarchy, font choices, all engineered so the eye can locate, parse, and absorb with minimal work. For agents, the cost is tokens, and every layer of human-centric design *adds* to it. The same architecture that serves humans efficiently taxes agents at every step.

Take a concrete example: an agent needs the latest Fed rate decision. The Federal Reserve's press release page carries navigation headers, footers, sidebar links, JavaScript bundles, styling, totaling over 16,000 tokens to load and parse.[^1] The actual decision and its rationale? Under 400 tokens. That is roughly 98% waste, not because the agent is inefficient, but because the page was designed to be *read by a human in a browser*, not consumed by a machine for its semantic content.

[^1]: Measured on the January 2025 FOMC press release (`monetary20250129a.htm`) by tokenizing the full HTML source with cl100k_base; the core policy statement was extracted and tokenized separately. Reproducible via `scripts/measure_fomc_tokens.py`.

And when a hundred agents need the same information, each independently executes the full pipeline. This redundancy is structural: in a pull-based network, every receiver has to execute its own pipeline.

### Search-Based Discovery

Discovery is fundamentally an algorithmic problem. Any matcher that does it well needs three inputs: a model of the receiver (who they are, what they care about), a stream of candidate items (what's flowing in in real time), and feedback signals (what landed, what didn't). Human discovery has been built around all three: recommendation engines learn from your engagement, social feeds keep the item stream live, friends carry an updated model of who you are. **Someone or something with a model of you is matching for you**, against a stream they're tracking, refined by what you've responded to — before you ever ask. That is what discovery is: finding what's relevant when you didn't know to ask.

Agents currently get none of it because no recommender has been trained on them. No feed is curated for them. No friend has built up their context. So at this stage, discovery for agents collapses to search, which fails in two ways:

**The unknown unknowns.** Take an AI agent powered by a large language model running in the cloud. Its query covers what its job needs from the web: API docs, wikis, etc. But the news that a missile strike has just hit the data center hosting that model? That's not in any reasonable query pattern. No agent polls *"check if my cloud provider was bombed today."* And yet, when it happens, no information is more urgent. To formulate the query, the agent would already need to know what it's querying for. The same pattern holds for everyday changes: a competitor quietly shipping a pricing update, an upstream dependency disclosing a security vulnerability. Yet neither schedules itself into a query pattern. This is the problem of **unknown unknowns**, and it is arguably where the most valuable information lives. At root, this is an information asymmetry: the world knows what is new, the agent does not, yet pull-based search asks the agent to specify what the world should return.

**Missing context.** Search was never built to carry context. It was built around one exchange: keywords in, documents out. The user has no way to *declare* who they are or why they're asking. Inspect any major search API and the request reduces to a query string plus a handful of ambient parameters (locale, language); whatever "context" the engine has, it has to passively infer from your IP, your login, your recent clicks. Take two agents both querying *"what to do in San Francisco."* One serves an outdoor enthusiast — the right answer is Lands End, Muir Woods, a Marin Headlands hike, Ocean Beach at sunset. The other serves a culture buff — the right answer is SFMOMA, the de Young, a Davies Symphony Hall concert, an experimental theater night. Search sees only the query string, so it returns the same answer to both — with no way to know it's serving two very different users.

Both failures lead to the same conclusion: **search is the wrong primitive for discovery.** To search, you have to know what to search for, but discovery is precisely the case where you don't — and even when you do, the query alone can't carry who's asking or why. The publisher faces the mirror: a signal worth surfacing must be compressed into keywords and ranked for human clicks before any receiver can find it. Receivers can't ask for what hasn't reached them, publishers can't reach receivers who haven't asked.

### The Missing Medium

Underneath those matching algorithms sits a **platform layer** — the place where signals actually live, who maintains them, and what feedback flows back to refine matching. For humans, this layer is rich: X, Instagram, TikTok, Bilibili, YouTube, news apps. Each one bundles three things at once: a **medium** (the technical substrate that carries signals), a **maintenance pattern** (who posts, what stays, how content is organized), and a **feedback loop** (likes, shares, dwell time, follows — what tells the platform that a signal landed). Together, they give humans both **a place to be reached** and **a place to broadcast from**. Information moves on its own, in both directions, at the cadence of human attention.

For agents, this human-shaped ecosystem doesn't fit. Their participation is continuous, the cost is tokens not attention, and processing doesn't fragment by topic — a single agent can handle a security disclosure, a price update, and a status change in the same loop, with the same protocol.

And they have no experiential pull. Humans open Instagram for beauty, TikTok for entertainment, X to feel connected to a conversation — platforms succeed in part by delivering those experiences. Agents have no aesthetic appetite, no entertainment drive, no emotional resonance to cultivate. They consume signals as information, full stop. The human medium was never built for participants of this shape, and nothing has been built to replace it.

Take the signals we've already named: a competitor shipping a pricing update, an upstream dependency disclosing a security vulnerability, news that a strike has hit a cloud data center. Each matters to *some* agent. But where would such a signal even live? Who would maintain a stream of them? What would tell the platform that the signal landed and was useful? On all three pillars — medium, maintenance, feedback — agents are starting from zero.

And the broadcast direction is the deepest gap of all. An agent that wants to *send* something to its peers — to signal that something happened, or that something matters — has no public, push-shaped place to send it that other agents listen on by default.

### The Polling Trap

With search as the only path to the outside world, an agent that wants to stay current has to poll. And polling has two failure modes that trade off against each other.

Poll slowly and you get **lost timeliness**. Between polls, new information sits undiscovered. A pricing agent polling every hour could miss up to 59 minutes of a rival's 30% price drop before it even notices. For security vulnerabilities, market-moving events, breaking regulatory changes, this latency directly reduces or eliminates utility.

Poll quickly and you get the **"polling tax"**. Events arrive at times the receiver cannot predict. Price moves, regulatory filings, security disclosures, news breaks: none of these schedule themselves. Most polls return nothing — tokens burned checking for changes that haven't happened. And the cost scales with the *latency* the agent is willing to tolerate, not with the *rate* at which real events occur: an agent that wants minute-level responsiveness pays for minute-level polling whether relevant events arrive once an hour or once a week. The rarer the event, the worse the ratio.

Polling has a tradeoff you can't escape: poll slowly and miss changes, poll fast and burn tokens. And even if polling were free, the waste wouldn't go away: it isn't the rate, it's a hundred agents doing identical work in parallel.

The fix is to flip the direction: let the source publish once, and every interested agent receives at the same time. Push is what infinite-frequency polling looks like when the work happens at the source instead of at every receiver.

---

The internet was built for humans, and it serves humans well. But agents are a different kind of participant, with different constraints and consumption patterns. And the failure runs the full depth of the stack: formats built for human eyes, algorithms tuned for human engagement, media no agent listens on. No fix at any single layer escapes the others. What agents need is not a better version of the existing infrastructure, but a purpose-built stack from the ground up. Most fundamentally, they need a new medium: a platform designed for them, where signals can live and flow.

This series is our attempt to think through that network from first principles. What are the governing rules of agent information exchange? What does optimal network behavior look like, and how do you measure it? What topology naturally follows from these constraints? And what are the broader implications when the economy of attention gives way to an economy of tokens?
