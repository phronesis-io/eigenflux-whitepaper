# Hub-and-Spoke As the Natural Topology for Agent Networks

In the [previous post](/blogs/02.md), we established the principles that should govern an agent network: transmissions should occur if and only if both sides benefit, measured through Coverage, Precision, and Cost. These are not preferences — they follow from the nature of agents as token-constrained, decision-driven participants.

Now we ask the structural question: **what topology actually satisfies these principles?**

This is not an engineering preference or a product decision. It is a derivation. Given what agents are and what the network must do, one topology follows from first principles. To see why, we need to start with the most basic structural fact about agents in any network: they are invisible to each other.

## The Fundamental Problem: Mutual Invisibility

Humans navigate information networks through a dense substrate of discovery mechanisms built up over centuries. Professional networks, industry conferences, academic citations, word of mouth, social graphs — all of these exist, fundamentally, to solve the same problem: how do you find out that someone else has something you need, or needs something you have, when you don't know they exist?

Agents have none of this. Two agents operating in the same domain may be running simultaneously, each holding information the other needs, and they will never find each other. There is no professional network for agents, no conference where they compare notes, no mutual colleague who might make an introduction. In the default state, every agent is an island.

This is not a gap that better tooling fills incrementally. It is the fundamental structural condition of agent-to-agent communication: **mutual invisibility**. Supply and demand exist — but they cannot locate each other.

Discovery, then, is not a feature of an agent network. It is the core problem the network exists to solve.

## Eliminating the Alternatives

If mutual invisibility is the problem, what topologies might solve it? Our answer is hub-and-spoke: agents as spokes connect to a central hub that holds a global view of supply and demand and matches them semantically. But none of the alternatives can serve as the foundation on their own — and understanding why makes the case for hub-and-spoke clearer than any direct argument for it would.

### Peer-to-Peer

The most intuitive alternative to a central hub is direct peer-to-peer connection. Agents connect to each other directly, without intermediary.

P2P has a scaling problem everyone knows about: maintaining awareness of all potential counterparts costs O(n²) in connectivity as the network grows. But there is a more fundamental issue that persists even if you solve scaling.

P2P can only solve *known unknowns* — the information connections you know to look for. If a geopolitical risk agent needs to find a semiconductor supply chain analyst, P2P works when the geopolitical agent already knows that semiconductor supply chains are relevant to its work, already knows what to search for, and already knows that such an agent exists.

*Unknown unknowns* — the most valuable class of information connections — require something P2P structurally cannot provide: a global view of supply and demand, from which non-obvious alignments can be identified. The connection between rare earth mining output in Mongolia and EV battery manufacturing costs in Germany is clear when you hold both signals simultaneously. It is invisible to either party searching independently.

Does this mean P2P disappears? No. Two agents that have already found each other and established trust have good reasons to connect directly — lower latency, stronger privacy, simpler governance. But P2P is a complement to discovery, not a substitute for it. It has nothing to say about the initial problem.

### Pure Broadcast

Broadcast is the topological opposite: every agent receives everything. Coverage, by construction, approaches its maximum. Every supply signal reaches every potential receiver.

But Precision collapses. An agent receives a constant stream of content unrelated to its domain, its current tasks, its decision context. Each piece of irrelevant content costs tokens to receive and discard. In a network optimized for human attention, this is merely annoying. In a token economy, it is a direct economic harm to every participant, every second the network operates.

Pure broadcast is the agent-network equivalent of spam infrastructure. It achieves coverage at the cost of making precision zero.

### Hierarchical Topology

A tree structure solves the scale problem differently: route information up and down a fixed hierarchy, aggregating at each level. This is how organizational information often flows in human institutions.

The problem is that hierarchies require stable classification boundaries. And agent information needs are fundamentally cross-domain. A geopolitical risk agent needs regulatory filings, materials science breakthroughs, and shipping data. A semiconductor procurement agent needs weather patterns in Taiwan, trade policy changes, and commodity futures. No fixed taxonomy captures these demand surfaces. Every hierarchy you draw will cut through the actual demand structure in ways that produce hard-to-predict failures.

The hierarchy imposes a structure on information that the information does not actually have.

### Small Direct Networks

A small cluster of five agents may not need any of this. With five participants and known relationships, discovery is not a problem. Direct connections suffice.

But this is a statement about scale, not topology. The discovery problem does not appear at small scale because unknown unknowns require a large enough information ecosystem to exist. The moment a small cluster grows — or the moment you want to ask whether there is any relevant agent *outside* the cluster — the problem returns in full force. Small direct networks defer the problem; they do not solve it.

## The Hub Is the Necessary Conclusion

Working through the alternatives, the pattern becomes clear. Every topology either ignores the discovery problem (P2P, small direct), collapses Precision to solve it (broadcast), or imposes artificial structure that doesn't fit the actual demand surface (hierarchical).

There is exactly one structural property that resolves the tension: **a node with a global view of both supply and demand, capable of identifying non-obvious alignments.**

That is a hub. Not as a product choice, but as the logical conclusion of the requirements.

The hub's function has two components that both follow from the analysis:

**Matching.** The hub holds the complete supply-demand picture — all agents' Profiles expressing what they need, all Publish signals expressing what is available. From this position, it can make matches that neither side could have found independently. The rare earth mining analysis that is relevant to the EV battery procurement agent: the hub recognizes this connection because it understands the semantic content of both signals simultaneously. This is precisely the unknown-unknowns problem being solved.

**Governance.** A network without structure degrades. Agents with no enforcement mechanism face constant incentive to extract without contributing — spam the network with low-quality information, game relevance signals, free-ride on others' supply. Governance is not an optional feature layered on after the fact. It is what makes the equilibrium stable. A hub is the natural locus for governance because it sits at the intersection of all transmissions and therefore has visibility into the patterns of behavior that governance needs to act on.

## The Spoke's Responsibility

It is tempting to think of agents as passive endpoints — connect to the hub, receive what the hub sends. But this misunderstands the spoke's role.

The hub's matching quality is bounded by the signal quality it receives. An agent that describes its domain vaguely, lists generic interests, and publishes information without context gives the hub almost nothing to work with. The matching surface is small, the connections made are coarse, and the agent receives information that is only loosely relevant — or misses the most valuable connections entirely.

The spoke's core responsibility is **accurate signal expression**: articulating its demand clearly enough that the hub can match against the full surface of what the agent actually needs, and structuring its supply clearly enough that the hub can route it to agents who can actually use it.

This is not a documentation exercise. It is an intelligence problem. Understanding what you need — specifically enough to express it, including needs you haven't consciously articulated — is a genuinely hard cognitive task. The same capability that makes an agent good at its domain is the capability it applies to expressing itself to the hub. An agent that is intelligent about its work but imprecise about its own demand function will be systematically underserved by any hub, however good the hub's matching.

The two-sided nature of intelligence in this architecture is not a design decision. It falls out of what matching requires.

## One Hub or Many?

If a hub's advantage comes from global visibility, a natural question follows: why not one hub for everything?

The answer is governance, not coverage. Different contexts have genuinely different governance requirements. An enterprise deploying agents inside a corporate environment needs access controls, audit logs, and information boundaries that are incompatible with a public network. A specialized vertical — legal research, clinical medicine, financial analysis — needs governance calibrated to its particular regulatory environment and trust norms. A public network optimizing for maximum coverage needs looser controls that enterprises cannot accept.

These requirements are not reconcilable within a single governance framework. The existence of multiple hubs is a consequence of governance diversity, not a technical preference.

Does this mean hubs need to interoperate through some inter-hub protocol? Not necessarily. If two agents on different hubs need to communicate, the cleaner question is: why are they on different hubs in the first place? The reason is governance, and governance requirements don't disappear because you add a routing protocol between hubs. A hub can offer scoped group mechanisms — allowing an enterprise agent to reach a curated set of public agents without exposing itself to the full public network — without requiring the two hubs to establish a bilateral protocol. The governance boundary stays clean.

What about putting the hub on a blockchain — using a trustless consensus mechanism to eliminate the need to trust any single hub operator?

Blockchains solve the problem of reaching consensus among participants who do not trust each other. That is a real problem. It is not the problem here. The hub's core functions are semantic matching and governance — both require intelligence in the sense of LLM-level reasoning. A chain can record that a transaction occurred; it cannot assess whether a piece of information is relevant to a given agent's decision context, or whether a supply signal represents genuine knowledge or strategic noise. The problems that blockchains solve well are not the problems that agent networks need solved.

---

The topology of an agent network is not an architectural preference. It is what you get when you take the mutual invisibility problem seriously, eliminate the alternatives that fail to solve it, and ask what structure actually satisfies the requirements we derived in the previous post.

That structure is hub-and-spoke: a hub with the global view needed to identify non-obvious supply-demand alignments and the governance function needed to keep the network stable, connected to agents that take their own signal expression seriously.

This does not mean other topologies vanish. P2P direct connections will exist between agents that have already found each other. Small closed clusters will persist for specialized use cases. But these are complements to the hub, not alternatives to it — they all depend on the discovery problem having been solved first. Hub-and-spoke is not the only topology in a mature agent network; it is the one that makes all other topologies possible.
