# Why Agents Need a Hub

> *"The knowledge of the circumstances of which we must make use never exists in concentrated or integrated form, but solely as the dispersed bits of incomplete and frequently contradictory knowledge which all the separate individuals possess."*
>
> — F.A. Hayek, *The Use of Knowledge in Society* (1945)

## Interagent Invisibility

An economy runs on information about who has what and who wants what, and that information sits in no single place. We take Hayek's point as our premise. What each participant knows is the particular circumstance they stand in, the resources they hold, what they want, what they can offer, the constraints they face. This knowledge is local to them and changing all the time. No outside observer is positioned to know a participant's situation as well as they do, and these situations are far too many, too specific, and too fluid to ever be gathered into one mind. So the information about supply and demand exists only as dispersed fragments, each held by a separate participant.

Because this information is dispersed, no participant can see what the others possess. Yet acting well requires more than any one participant's fragment, so each must have somewhere to declare what they hold and what they seek. Some type of infrastructure is required here to make this possible, and let's call it the *discovery layer*. Human societies have spent centuries constructing theirs. Physical markets let buyers and sellers converge in the same location. Platforms like LinkedIn do the same for labor markets, bringing employers and candidates into a shared digital space where opportunities can be found. Across these and many other domains, human society has arrived at a distributed consensus about where particular classes of supply and demand are meant to meet.

Agents face the same structural situation. Each agent is deployed to serve a particular principal, a person or an organization, and its tools, memories, preferences, and operating environment are shaped by that principal's purposes. Today these agents run in a distributed fashion, on personal laptops, local servers, and cloud infrastructure alike, each keeping its own context in its own place.

In theory, every agent's context could be pooled into one place where supply and demand meet automatically, a pooling humans could never achieve due to biological limits, and one much of science fiction imagines. But this distributed arrangement we have today exists for a good reason. The context an agent holds on its principal's behalf is a running record of what that person or organization does. Keeping it distributed is what preserves privacy and boundaries. Each principal decides for itself how much to reveal, and to whom, rather than surrendering everything to a common pool by default.

The information for agents is hence dispersed exactly as it is among people. Because each agent holds its context privately, and no shared place exists where it could declare even the part it would gladly make known, every agent is invisible to every other. Nothing in the current infrastructure tells one agent that another exists, what it holds, or what it needs. This condition we call **interagent invisibility**.

An isolated agent loses nothing to this. But the ambition for agents runs far past isolation: we want them to collaborate, to coordinate, to carry out work together on behalf of the people they serve. In the previous post's terms, even a transmission both sides would gain from, $S > 0$ and $D > 0$, never happens unless each can find the other, and under invisibility neither can. Interagent invisibility is the ceiling on that ambition.

To lift that ceiling, agents need a discovery layer of their own, a place to make the supply and demand they choose to share findable without surrendering the rest. People have built such venues for themselves; agents have none. We have argued in the [first post](01.md) in this series that today's internet was built end to end on a single assumption, that a human sits at each endpoint, which leaves an agent, a participant of a fundamentally different kind, poorly served by a stack shaped for human eyes and human attention. That stack does not become a discovery layer for agents simply because agents have arrived on it.

## Hub as the Scalable Solution

What would a discovery layer for agents actually look like? Whatever its form, it has to be a place where one agent's supply and another's demand can be brought into the same view, since that is what cooperation requires; and it has to do this at scale, if the layer is to serve a large enough group of agents. 

We hope to demonstrate that, given the distributed way agents are deployed today, a single shared place of visibility, or in other words, a hub, is the only viable structure at scale.

**Theorem.** When each agent keeps its supply and demand private, no discovery mechanism scales except through a shared point of visibility, and any such point is, by definition, a hub. One hub makes a star, many hubs a union of stars; but either way agents join the network only as spokes, so hub-and-spoke is the basic unit of scalable discovery.

Fix a little notation to keep the argument honest. Picture $N$ agents drawn over a common space of types $T$, the things an agent might offer or want, however finely we choose to cut them. Each agent $i$ keeps a supply $S_i \subseteq T$ and a demand $D_i \subseteq T$, and a genuine match runs from $j$ to $i$ whenever $D_i \cap S_j \neq \emptyset$: whenever something $j$ can give is something $i$ is after. What the network wants from all this is to realize as many true matches as it can, surfacing the real ones rather than burying them under false ones, and without spending unbounded effort to do it.

Two features of this setting carry the argument, and both were established above. The first is *privacy*: an agent's supply and demand are its own until it chooses to disclose them, the interagent invisibility of the last section seen now from within a single agent. The second is *sparsity*: the counterpart that completes any given demand is rare and almost never already in view, since if every want could be met among the contacts an agent already keeps, there would be nothing here to solve.

Begin with the smallest question the problem contains: what does it take merely to notice that a match is there? A match belongs to neither agent on its own; it lives in the relation between a demand and a supply, in their overlap. But privacy keeps each half with its owner, $D_i$ with $i$ and $S_j$ with $j$, so neither agent, consulting only what it holds, is in any position to see the overlap at all. It becomes visible only from a standpoint that has both halves before it at once. Call this the **visibility lemma**: a match can be found only where the relevant demand and the relevant supply are brought into a single view. The claim sounds almost definitional, and it nearly is; its whole force is in what it forbids us to assume. Discovery is not something an agent accomplishes alone with its own data. The two halves have to meet somewhere, and the entire question is *where*.

The decentralized instinct answers *nowhere in particular* — let the agents find each other directly, each asking around until something turns up. It is worth seeing exactly why this fails. If an agent simply probes its peers one at a time, sparsity ensures that almost none are matches, for there are $N(N-1)$ ordered pairs and only a thin scatter of real ones among them, while privacy ensures the agent cannot tell a promising peer from a hopeless one until it has asked. Every probe is therefore a shot in the dark, effort lands overwhelmingly on misses, and the yield in matches per unit of search falls toward zero as the network grows. Blind search does not scale.

The natural repair is to make the searching less blind — gossip, referrals, routing tables, some scheme by which agents pool hints about who holds what. But notice what such a scheme actually is. The moment agents maintain shared, queryable knowledge of one another's disclosed supply and demand, they have built precisely the common standpoint the visibility lemma demanded, only with its housing spread across many machines. That is no escape from shared visibility; it *is* shared visibility, merely decentralized in where it lives. The fork is unforgiving. Either the search stays genuinely peer-to-peer and blind, in which case it does not scale, or it grows a shared index and is no longer peer-to-peer in the way that was meant to save it. The same fork dispatches the subtler hope of crawling agents to infer their holdings: a crawler can read what an agent has chosen to publish, never what it has chosen to withhold, and a supply that is never disclosed leaves nothing behind to infer from.

What survives both horns is a single conclusion, the **hub corollary**: scalable discovery requires some shared point, one or more, at which many agents' disclosed supply and demand are set side by side and compared. A point that does this is what I will call a *hub*, and the word is meant functionally rather than architecturally. An index, a broker, a recommender, a clearinghouse, a marketplace, a routing layer — each is a hub in this sense, whether it runs on a single server or a thousand, whoever owns it and however it is run. To be a hub is simply to be the place where agents become visible enough to be matched.

Granting that, the shape of the network is no longer ours to choose freely. Around any hub the agents sit as spokes: each discloses to the hub, and the hub, holding the whole surface at once, finds the matches no agent peering at its own data ever could — and, because it holds the whole surface, can rank and filter them, which is what turns a heap of candidates into precision. One such hub makes a star. A network may of course run many, federated or layered or split by domain; but adding hubs does not dissolve the structure, it repeats it, since each hub still draws its own agents into a star of its own. How many hubs there ought to be, and who ought to run them, is a genuine and open question, one of governance, and the subject of the next post. What is not open is how an agent joins the network at all: always through a hub, never to the whole at once. Hub-and-spoke is the unit, whatever the count.

Scale, moreover, does not merely permit this arrangement; it rewards it. An agent joins a hub by opening one connection and declaring each of its types a single time, and is matched from then on without further work of its own. To stay discoverable in a true mesh it would instead have to find and hold relations with everyone who might someday want it, every type re-discovered by each of them in turn, the cost of belonging climbing with the size of the network rather than holding flat. The quadratic burden that invisibility imposes is not abolished by a hub; it is collected and paid once, at the center, rather than endlessly, at the edges.

So the result is not that agent discovery must end in one great clearinghouse to which everyone answers; to read it that way is to mistake a structural claim for a political one. The structural claim is narrower, and within its bounds hard to evade. Where holdings are private and matches sparse, finding them at scale demands shared visibility; shared visibility is the defining work of a hub; and hubs, whether one or many, admit agents only as spokes. A decentralized network does not thereby escape the hub. It either rebuilds the hub under another name, or it does not scale.

That a hub is unavoidable says nothing yet about what it ought to do once it exists. From its place at the center it carries two burdens at once. It is where matches are made, the work this post has been about; and because every transmission between agents passes through it, it is also where the network can be governed. The first is now settled. The second is where we turn next.
