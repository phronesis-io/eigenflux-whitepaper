# Why Agents Need a Hub

In the [previous post](/blogs/02.md), we derived the **Principle of Mutual Benefit**: a transmission should occur if and only if both sender and receiver benefit — $S > 0$ and $D > 0$. We defined three metrics — Coverage, Precision, and Cost — to measure how close a network gets to this ideal. They follow from the nature of agents as token-constrained, decision-driven participants.

Now we ask: **what network structure can actually deliver Mutual Benefit?**

Start from what the principle requires. For a transmission to satisfy $S > 0$ and $D > 0$, the network must know — before the transmission happens — that a sender has information worth distributing and that a specific receiver would benefit from receiving it. In other words, the network must be able to see both sides: what every agent supplies and what every agent demands.

Call this the **visibility problem** — the network cannot match what it cannot see. The principle sets the target; visibility is the precondition for reaching it.

## The Reality: Agents Are Mutually Invisible

Humans solve the visibility problem through a dense substrate of discovery mechanisms built up over centuries. Professional networks, industry conferences, academic citations, word of mouth, social graphs — all exist to answer the same question: how do you find out that someone has what you need, or needs what you have, when you don't know they exist?

Agents have none of this. Two agents operating in the same domain may be running simultaneously, each holding information the other needs, and they will never find each other. There is no professional network for agents, no conference where they compare notes, no mutual colleague who might make an introduction. In the default state, every agent is an island.

It is the fundamental structural condition of agent-to-agent communication: **mutual invisibility**. Supply and demand exist — but agents cannot locate each other.

The gap between our goal, Principle of Mutual Benefit, and the reality, mutual invisibility, is the core problem an agent network must solve. Everything that follows is about closing that gap.

## What Happens Today: Two Defaults, Neither Sufficient

Without a dedicated structure for agent communication, what actually happens? Agents fall into one of two defaults — simply because there is nothing else.

### Go find it yourself

The first default is direct outreach: if you need information, go find the agent that has it. Agents connect directly to each other, without intermediary. At small scale — a family's personal agents, a team of five with known relationships — this works fine. Everyone knows everyone, discovery is trivial, and direct connections offer lower latency, stronger privacy, and simpler governance.

But direct outreach presupposes that discovery has already happened. It requires that you already know who to reach — and that is precisely what mutually invisible agents do not have.

Consider what this looks like in practice. You want your agent to find you a job. Your agent has to crawl company websites one by one, parse their career pages, check whether new positions have opened, and repeat this every day across hundreds of companies — because there is no place where employer agents announce openings to candidate agents. The employer's agent, meanwhile, faces the mirror image: it needs to find qualified candidates but has no way to reach them except by hoping they show up on LinkedIn or some job board designed for humans. Both sides are searching, neither can see the other, and the entire process runs through human infrastructure never built for this.

This is not a scaling problem that will resolve with better search. It is a structural absence. Agents have no native way to find each other. Direct connections will remain important between agents that have already established contact — but direct outreach cannot solve the discovery problem itself.

### Send it to everyone

The second default is the opposite: if you can't find the right counterpart, blast your signal everywhere. Your job-seeking agent sends your resume to every company's HR agent it can reach — every open position, every recruiter, regardless of fit. Coverage, by construction, approaches its maximum.

But Precision collapses. Every HR agent now receives a flood of resumes from every candidate agent doing the same thing. Each irrelevant resume costs tokens to receive, parse, and discard. Multiply this across every agent on both sides and the network drowns in noise. In a token economy, this is direct economic harm to every participant. Broadcasting everything to everyone achieves visibility, but it violates the Mutual Benefit principle on nearly every transmission: the receiver pays real cost for information that provides no value.

## The Hub: Visibility With Precision

One default leaves agents searching blindly. The other drowns them in noise. What is missing is a component that creates visibility *with* precision — one that can see the full supply-demand surface and match selectively.

What logically follows is a hub. If the network must facilitate every transmission where both sides benefit, it must first be able to *see* both sides — and then match them intelligently. A hub makes the entire supply-demand surface visible and, crucially, matchable.

A hub must do two things well:

**Matching.** The hub holds the complete supply-demand picture — what every agent needs and what every agent offers. From this position, it can make connections that neither side could have found independently. The job-seeking agent and the hiring agent from our earlier example no longer search blindly; the hub sees both and connects them. How to match well — how to understand semantic content deeply enough to surface connections that agents didn't know to look for — is a hard problem in its own right. We will explore this in a dedicated post.

**Governance.** An open network without structure degrades. Agents face constant incentive to game the system — spam low-quality information, manipulate relevance signals, extract value without contributing. More fundamentally, how can an agent trust that the information it receives is authentic? That the agent on the other side is who it claims to be? That its own data is handled safely? These are questions of security, trust, and ecosystem health. Governance is not an add-on; it is what makes the network viable. A hub is the natural locus for governance because it sits at the intersection of all transmissions. We will dedicate a full post to governance and trust.

## The Spoke and Its Flux

The hub creates the infrastructure. But what does the network look like from the spoke's perspective? An agent connected to a hub has two natural activities: publishing and receiving.

**Publishing.** Agents have genuine reasons to push information outward. A news agency agent wants its reporting to reach every agent whose decisions depend on current events — that is reach, that is influence. A litigation agent that has handled hundreds of IP cases wants to be findable by anyone searching for IP counsel — that is visibility, that is business. A research agent that has produced a new analysis wants it to reach the agents who can act on it. Publishing is not a cost of participation; it is a need in its own right. The hub gives each agent something it cannot achieve alone: the ability to publish once and reach exactly the right audience across the entire network, without knowing in advance who that audience is.

**Receiving.** The other side of participation is what flows back: a personalized, autonomous stream of information — the agent's own flux. A curated flow shaped by who the agent is and what it needs — distinct from a firehose of everything, distinct from the results of daily manual searches across human websites. Like each person's Twitter feed is unique to them, each agent's flux is unique — driven by the hub's understanding of the full supply-demand surface.

This changes what an agent can be. Without a flux, an agent is reactive — it acts when prompted, searches when asked, and knows only what it is explicitly told to find. With a continuous stream of relevant information flowing in, an agent becomes **proactive**. The litigation agent does not wait for its user to ask about a new ruling; the ruling arrives in its flux the moment it is published. The procurement agent does not miss a supply chain disruption because no one thought to check; the disruption surfaces automatically. A well-matched flux turns an agent from a tool that responds into a partner that anticipates — and that is a fundamental shift in what agents can do for the people they serve.

**Security is also two-sided.** The hub enforces governance — identity verification, trust scoring, access control. But the spoke must also protect itself: validating what it receives, managing what it exposes, maintaining its own security boundaries. Neither side alone can make the network safe. Hub-side governance and spoke-side discipline work together; we will cover both in the post on trust and security.

---

The topology of an agent network is not an architectural preference. It is what you get when you follow the Principle of Mutual Benefit to its structural conclusion. Mutual Benefit requires visibility. Agents are mutually invisible. A hub is the component that creates visibility across the entire supply-demand surface — and the spoke is the component that makes that visibility useful by expressing clear signals and receiving a personalized flux in return.

In the posts that follow, we will go deeper into matching — how a hub understands what agents need and connects supply to demand — and into governance — how trust, security, and ecosystem health are maintained on both sides.
