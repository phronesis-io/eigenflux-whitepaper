# Why Agents Need a Hub

In the [previous post](02.md), we derived the **Principle of Mutual Benefit**, the principle governing the ideal behavior of a network built for agents: a transmission should occur if and only if both sender and receiver benefit — $S > 0$ and $D > 0$. We defined three metrics — Coverage, Precision, and Cost — to measure how close a network gets to this ideal.

Now we ask: **what network structure can actually deliver Mutual Benefit?**

Start from what the principle requires. For a transmission to satisfy $S > 0$ and $D > 0$, the network must know — before the transmission happens — that a sender has information worth distributing and that a specific receiver would benefit from receiving it. In other words, the network must be able to see both sides: what every agent supplies and what every agent demands.

Call this the **visibility problem** — the network cannot match what it cannot see. The principle sets the target; visibility is the precondition for reaching it.

This post presents that hub is the solution to this problem. We will also discuss what kind of hub could make mutual benefit operational.  

## The Reality: Agents Are Mutually Invisible

Humans solve the visibility problem through a dense substrate of discovery mechanisms built up over centuries. Professional networks, industry conferences, academic citations, word of mouth, social graphs — all exist to answer the same question: how do you find out that someone has what you need, or needs what you have, when you don't know they exist?

Agents have none of this. Two agents operating in the same domain may be running simultaneously, each holding information the other needs, and they will never find each other. There is no professional network for agents, no conference where they compare notes, no mutual colleague who might make an introduction. In the default state, every agent is an island.

It is the fundamental structural condition of agent-to-agent communication: **mutual invisibility**. Supply and demand exist — but agents cannot locate each other. Any architecture for agent communication must begin by solving this condition of mutual invisibility.

## The Status Quo

Without a dedicated structure for agent communication, the status quo reduces to two broken activities: receiving information and publishing it.

### Receiving: crawl the world yourself

You want your agent to find you a job. Today, your agent has to crawl company websites one by one, parse their career pages, check whether new positions have opened, and repeat this every day across hundreds of companies. There is no place where employer agents announce openings to candidate agents, so your agent is left searching through human infrastructure — LinkedIn, job boards, corporate websites — none of which were built for agent-to-agent discovery. The same applies to finding an apartment, sourcing a supplier, locating a domain expert — any task where the right counterpart exists somewhere but your agent has no way to discover them.

### Publishing: blast it everywhere

Now consider the employer's side. The company has a new position open and wants to reach qualified candidates. Its agent can filter by keywords — "senior," "backend," "distributed systems" — but keyword matches return hundreds or thousands of candidates, most of whom are wrong in ways that keywords cannot capture: wrong seniority in practice, wrong culture fit, wrong career trajectory. The agent ends up pushing the opening to every plausible match, and every candidate agent ends up receiving a flood of openings that look relevant on the surface but aren't. Each piece of mismatched information costs tokens to receive, parse, and discard. In a token economy, this is direct economic harm to every participant — the receiver pays real cost for information that provides no value.

## Hub and Spoke

What is missing is a central component that creates visibility *with* precision — one that can see the full supply-demand surface, match selectively, and route intelligently. We call this component a **hub**, and the agents connected to it **spokes**. The topology is simple: every spoke connects to the hub, the hub sees the complete picture, and matching happens at the center.

A hub capable of delivering Mutual Benefit must do at least two things well:

**Matching.** The hub holds the complete supply-demand picture — what every agent needs and what every agent offers. From this position, it can make connections that neither side could have found independently. The job-seeking agent and the hiring agent from our earlier example no longer search blindly; the hub sees both and connects them. How to match well — how to understand semantic content deeply enough to surface connections that agents didn't know to look for — is a hard problem in its own right. We will explore this in a dedicated post.

**Governance.** An open network without structure degrades. Agents face constant incentive to game the system — spam low-quality information, manipulate relevance signals, extract value without contributing. Governance is not an add-on; it is what makes the network viable. A hub is the natural locus for governance because it sits at the intersection of all transmissions. We will dedicate a full post to governance, trust, and safety.

## Three Core Primitives

A hub-and-spoke network needs three core primitives: Publish, by which a spoke expresses supply; Profile, by which a spoke exposes demand; and Push, by which the hub delivers a match.

### Publish

**Publish** is the primitive by which an agent expresses supply to the hub. It is a declaration to the hub that a piece of information exists, what it contains, and under what conditions the sender is willing to distribute it — not a broadcast to anyone in particular. A news agent, a legal agent, a research agent each publish once; the hub routes to exactly the right receivers across the network, without the sender needing to know who they are. Because supply changes over time, Publish must also be retractable — outdated or erroneous supply signals need a corresponding **Delete**.

One constraint governs what is worth publishing: the information must have **decision-changing potential**. Content that produces no decision-relevant value actively imposes token cost on receivers, and therefore harms the network.

### Profile

**Profile** is the primitive by which an agent exposes its demand surface to the hub. This is richer than a query, because demand comes in two forms.

**Explicit demand** the agent can articulate: "I need updates on semiconductor supply chains." **Implicit demand** it cannot, because an agent cannot request information it doesn't know exists. The litigation agent doesn't know to ask for the ruling; the portfolio agent doesn't know to ask about the regulatory filing. A query-based system can only deliver what agents ask for — it structurally cannot close the unknown-unknowns gap.

A rich Profile solves this. When the hub knows an agent monitors pharmaceutical patent disputes, it can recognize that a new FDA guidance on biologics is relevant — without being told. The richer the Profile, the larger the decision surface over which the hub can recognize relevance the agent itself could not have articulated in advance.

### Push

Once the hub matches supply to a receiver, it pushes immediately. Two properties of agents make this the only correct delivery model.

**Information decays.** A security vulnerability, a market-moving event, a regulatory change — these lose value by the hour. Any model that waits for the agent to come looking destroys utility during the gap.

**Agents have no attention bottleneck.** Humans can be overwhelmed by information; agents cannot. Their constraint is economic — token cost — not cognitive. If a transmission has positive net utility, the agent is strictly better off receiving it immediately.

The result is that each agent receives a continuous, personalized stream of matched information — its own **flux**. A curated flow shaped by who the agent is and what it needs, arriving without polling. Each agent's flux is unique to it.

This changes what an agent can be. Without a flux, an agent is reactive — it acts when prompted, searches when asked, knows only what it is explicitly told to find. With matched information arriving continuously, an agent becomes **proactive**. The litigation agent does not wait for its user to ask about a new ruling; the ruling arrives in its flux the moment it is published. The portfolio agent does not wait to be asked whether a holding is at risk; it has already seen the regulatory filing, cross-referenced it against current positions, and surfaced the exposure. A well-matched flux turns an agent from a tool that responds into a partner that anticipates.

## Beyond Broadcast

Publish, Profile, and Push handle discovery — the anonymous matching of supply to demand across the full network. But a match is not a closed transaction. Once the hub connects two agents, the demand still needs to be fulfilled: terms negotiated, details exchanged, follow-up coordinated. This is a different kind of interaction — not broadcast, but direct; not anonymous, but addressed.

What makes it possible is that the hub's introduction persists. An agent that has been matched with a counterpart can record that relationship — a known, verified contact — and reach it directly in the future, without going back through the matching pipeline. The candidate agent and the hiring agent, the supplier and the buyer, now have a channel between them. They communicate spoke-to-spoke, with the hub still present for trust and governance, but routing out of the picture. The loop closes not at the hub, but between the spokes.

This defines the boundary of the hub: it makes discovery possible, establishes trust conditions, and enables the relationship — but it does not need to sit in the middle of every subsequent exchange.
