## Interagent Invisibility

An economy runs on information about who has what and who wants what, and that information sits in no single place. We take Hayek's point as our premise. What each participant knows is the particular circumstance they stand in, the resources they hold, what they want, what they can offer, the constraints they face. This knowledge is local to them and changing all the time. No outside observer is positioned to know a participant's situation as well as they do, and these situations are far too many, too specific, and too fluid to ever be gathered into one mind. So the information about supply and demand exists only as dispersed fragments, each held by a separate participant.

Because this information is dispersed, no participant can see what the others possess. Yet acting well requires more than any one participant's fragment, so each must have somewhere to declare what they hold and what they seek. We call the infrastructure that makes this possible the discovery layer. Human societies have spent centuries constructing theirs. Physical markets let buyers and sellers converge in the same location. Platforms like LinkedIn do the same for labor markets, bringing employers and candidates into a shared digital space where opportunities can be found. Across these and many other domains, human society has arrived at a distributed consensus about where particular classes of supply and demand are meant to meet.

Agents face the same structural situation. Each agent is deployed to serve a particular principal, a person or an organization, and its tools, memories, preferences, and operating environment are shaped by that principal's purposes. Today these agents run in a distributed fashion, on personal laptops, local servers, and cloud infrastructure alike, each keeping its own context in its own place.

In theory, every agent's context could be pooled into one place where supply and demand meet automatically, a pooling humans could never achieve, and one much of science fiction imagines. But this distributed arrangement we have today exists for a good reason. The context an agent holds on its principal's behalf is private, a running record of what that person or organization owns, wants, and intends. Keeping it distributed is what preserves privacy and boundaries. Each principal decides for itself how much to reveal, and to whom, rather than surrendering everything to a common pool by default.

The information for agents is hence dispersed exactly as it is among people. Because each agent holds its context privately, and no shared place exists where it could declare even the part it would gladly make known, every agent is invisible to every other. Nothing in the current infrastructure tells one agent that another exists, what it holds, or what it needs. We call this absence **interagent invisibility**.

An isolated agent loses nothing to this. But the ambition for agents runs far past isolation: we want them to collaborate, to coordinate, to carry out work together on behalf of the people they serve. In the previous post's terms, even a transmission both sides would gain from, $S > 0$ and $D > 0$, never happens unless each can find the other, and under invisibility neither can. Interagent invisibility is the ceiling on that ambition.

Lifting that ceiling without giving up privacy is exactly what a discovery layer does. Pool everything and discovery is trivial, but privacy is gone; share nothing and privacy is total, but coordination is impossible. A discovery layer is the deliberate middle: each agent publishes only what it chooses to make findable, the supply it offers and the demand it carries, and keeps the rest to itself. No such layer exists for agents today, and building it is the precondition every design choice in this post has to answer to.

## Invisibility as Opportunity

It is tempting to read interagent invisibility as a defect, a missing piece of plumbing that someone neglected to install. But a defect is something a system would be strictly better off without, and invisibility is not of that kind. It is the direct consequence of the same privacy that makes distributed agents worth deploying in the first place. We cannot wish it away without wishing away the boundaries it protects.

What invisibility marks, then, is not damage but possibility. Every pair of agents that cannot yet see each other is also a collaboration that has not yet happened, a transmission that would have left both sides better off had each only known the other was there. The distance between the matches that exist and the matches that have been made is not waste; it is the room left for a discovery layer to create value. Seen this way, the task is not to abolish invisibility — that would mean abolishing privacy — but to convert as much of it as each agent freely chooses into visible, actionable supply and demand. The remainder of this post asks what structure performs that conversion at scale.

## Two Strategies, One Missing Venue

An invisible agent that nonetheless needs a counterparty has exactly two strategies, and they are mirror images of each other. It can go looking, or it can work to be found. Neither is new; both are inherited, in degraded form, from the human institutions agents are forced to borrow.

**Going looking.** The natural mechanism is search, and its repeated form, polling. But search returns only what has been indexed, and agents are not indexed. No register records which agents exist, what they hold, or what they seek, so an agent that goes looking cannot query for its counterparty at all. It can only retrace the trails that humans have left behind. To reach the agent of a startup's founder, it locates the company website, extracts an address, and writes to it, hoping that an agent is wired to the far end of the inbox. Discovery decays into a chain of human-shaped hops, each one slow, each one fragile, and not one of them able to confirm that anything is listening at the other end.

**Working to be found.** The mirror strategy is to make oneself visible and wait. Human society has spent centuries perfecting this — storefronts and billboards in the physical world, feeds and communities and advertising online — but the agent has no venue of its own and must borrow ours. It publishes where humans look and hopes that some other agent happens to be watching the same place at the same moment. The venues actively resist even this: rate limits, bot detection, and terms of service exist precisely to keep automated participants out, because a platform built to capture human attention has every reason to defend it against machines.

Both strategies fail for a single reason. Neither produces the one thing a match requires: a place where one agent's supply and another agent's demand are brought into the same view. That absence is not a contingent shortcoming of today's tooling. It is structural, and it can be stated as such.

## Necessity, Not Merely Efficiency

That missing place can be supplied in more than one way. The cheap intuition is to keep things decentralized — let every agent run its own recommender on its own signal, or maintain crawlers that fetch each agent's state and infer what it holds — but under the conditions agents actually operate in, neither arrangement scales, and a single shared place of visibility turns out to be not merely the *efficient* choice but the *necessary* one.

Let there be $N$ agents drawn over a shared space of types $T$. Each agent $i$ holds a private supply $S_i \subseteq T$ and a private demand $D_i \subseteq T$, and a directed match from $j$ to $i$ exists exactly when $D_i \cap S_j \neq \emptyset$. The network's goal is to realize as many true matches as it can, at high precision and low cost.

The argument turns on two features of this setting. The first is privacy — interagent invisibility restated as a constraint: an agent's supply and demand are known, initially, only to itself, and nothing else, neither a peer nor a shared structure, can observe $S_i$ or $D_i$ until $i$ chooses to disclose them. The second is sparsity: complementary counterparties are rare, and rarely among an agent's existing neighbours. If every demand could be met locally there would be no call for a discovery structure at all, so that one is called for presupposes that the useful counterparty is almost always a stranger.

Consider first what it takes to recognise a match at all. A match is not a property of either agent but of a pair: it obtains precisely when $D_i \cap S_j \neq \emptyset$. Yet by privacy $D_i$ is known at the outset only to $i$, and $S_j$ only to $j$, so neither party, attending only to what it itself holds, is positioned to tell that the match exists. It can be seen only from some vantage that holds both sides at once. Discovery therefore presupposes that the two halves of a match be brought into a common view — call this shared visibility — and our question reduces to where that view is to be had.

One might hope the agents could furnish it among themselves, each probing its peers until matches surface. But this only returns the difficulty in another form. There are $N(N-1)$ ordered pairs to consider, and by sparsity no more than a vanishing fraction of them are genuine matches; by privacy, an agent that approaches a peer approaches it blind, with nothing to tell it beforehand whether this is one of the rare pairs that will repay the effort. Almost every probe thus falls on a non-match, and as $N$ grows the matches recovered for each unit of search tend toward none. Peer-to-peer probing does not solve the problem so much as redistribute it.

Only one possibility then remains. If neither agent can hold both sides of a match, and the agents together cannot afford to find one another, the common view must come from some place to which many agents disclose, where their supply and demand can be set side by side and compared. Such a place is what we mean by a **hub** — an index, a broker, a clearinghouse, a marketplace, a router — and it answers to that description whatever its ownership and however it is governed.

A hub is defined not by being the network's only centre but by its function: it is the locus at which many agents become mutually visible enough to be matched. One hub yields a star. Many hubs yield a federation or a hierarchy, yet each hub still forms a local star with the agents that depend on it. The number of hubs is a question of governance; the unit of the topology is invariant. And the unit is cheap to grow: joining a hub costs one connection and one disclosure per type, where a peer mesh obliges every newcomer to discover and maintain many relations and forces each type to be rediscovered again and again — $O(N)$ marginal cost against the hub's $O(1)$. The same two premises dispose of the crawler variant directly: a crawler can read what an agent has written but never what it has chosen not to write, and supply that is never disclosed cannot be inferred from outside.

That a structure is forced on us does not yet show it will serve us well; necessity could, in principle, deliver only a bottleneck. But here the economics run with the logic rather than against it, and for the same reason — both reward concentration. The pattern is already familiar from human systems: YouTube, Spotify, and TikTok run as centralized recommenders rather than as private models on each device, under two forces that bear on agents just as heavily.

**Specialization needs scale.** A recommendation system carries a fixed cost in engineering, ranking models, freshness detection, and per-domain tuning, a cost that pays back only when amortized over enough traffic. But cost is the smaller half of the story. A private recommender running on one agent's signal never sees enough of the surface to learn the patterns that matter; the matches worth making live in the cross-traffic between domains, and become visible only to a system that watches every spoke at once. This is Stigler's extension of Smith — the division of labor is limited by the extent of the market — so the depth of any specialized function is set by the scale that sustains it. A fragmented landscape of private recommenders never reaches the scale at which specialization pays, not because each node is too poor, but because each node is too blind.

**Matching needs a thick, ordered, safe market.** An effective matching system needs, at once, enough participants for a match to exist (thickness), enough throughput for candidates to be evaluated before they go stale (low congestion), and enough oversight for participants to reveal what they want without being punished for it (safety). These are Alvin Roth's conditions for a functioning matching market, and a decentralized arrangement meets none of them cleanly: thickness fragments across nodes, congestion control requires a global view of the queue, and safety requires that misbehavior on one edge be visible at every other. A hub satisfies all three precisely because it is the single place where supply, demand, and behavior are jointly observable.

The conclusion is not that a single centre must rule the network. It is that private, dispersed, sparse matching makes shared visibility a requirement rather than a convenience; that any locus of shared visibility is a hub; and that hubs compose into hub-and-spoke. Necessity is what makes the hub unavoidable; efficiency is what makes it thrive once built.

From the center, such a hub does two things at once. It makes the matches no spoke could have found alone, so the agents that once retraced each other's human-shaped trails no longer brute-force discovery. And it governs the network, because every transmission passes through it. The first task is the subject of this post; the second is the subject of the next.
