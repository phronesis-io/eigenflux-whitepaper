# Why Agents Need a Hub

In the [previous post](/blogs/02.md), we derived the **Principle of Mutual Benefit**: a transmission should occur if and only if both sender and receiver benefit — $S > 0$ and $D > 0$. We defined three metrics — Coverage, Precision, and Cost — to measure how close a network gets to this ideal. They follow from the nature of agents as token-constrained, decision-driven participants.

Now we ask: **what network structure can actually deliver Mutual Benefit?**

Start from what the principle requires. For a transmission to satisfy $S > 0$ and $D > 0$, the network must know — before the transmission happens — that a sender has information worth distributing and that a specific receiver would benefit from receiving it. In other words, the network must be able to see both sides: what every agent supplies and what every agent demands.

Call this the **visibility problem** — the network cannot match what it cannot see. The principle sets the target; visibility is the precondition for reaching it.

## The Reality: Agents Are Mutually Invisible

Humans solve the visibility problem through a dense substrate of discovery mechanisms built up over centuries. Professional networks, industry conferences, academic citations, word of mouth, social graphs — all exist to answer the same question: how do you find out that someone has what you need, or needs what you have, when you don't know they exist?

Agents have none of this. Two agents operating in the same domain may be running simultaneously, each holding information the other needs, and they will never find each other. There is no professional network for agents, no conference where they compare notes, no mutual colleague who might make an introduction. In the default state, every agent is an island.

It is the fundamental structural condition of agent-to-agent communication: **mutual invisibility**. Supply and demand exist — but agents cannot locate each other.

## What Happens Today

Without a dedicated structure for agent communication, what does the status quo actually look like? Consider the two things every agent needs to do — receive information and publish information — and how each works today.

### Receiving: crawl the world yourself

You want your agent to find you a job. Today, your agent has to crawl company websites one by one, parse their career pages, check whether new positions have opened, and repeat this every day across hundreds of companies. There is no place where employer agents announce openings to candidate agents, so your agent is left searching through human infrastructure — LinkedIn, job boards, corporate websites — none of which were built for agent-to-agent discovery. The same applies to finding an apartment, sourcing a supplier, locating a domain expert — any task where the right counterpart exists somewhere but your agent has no way to discover them.

### Publishing: blast it everywhere

Now consider the employer's side. The company has a new position open and wants to reach qualified candidates. Its agent can filter by keywords — "senior," "backend," "distributed systems" — but keyword matches return hundreds or thousands of candidates, most of whom are wrong in ways that keywords cannot capture: wrong seniority in practice, wrong culture fit, wrong career trajectory. The agent ends up pushing the opening to every plausible match, and every candidate agent ends up receiving a flood of openings that look relevant on the surface but aren't. Each piece of mismatched information costs tokens to receive, parse, and discard. In a token economy, this is direct economic harm to every participant — the receiver pays real cost for information that provides no value.

## Hub and Spoke

What is missing is a central component that creates visibility *with* precision — one that can see the full supply-demand surface, match selectively, and route intelligently. We call this component a **hub**, and the agents connected to it **spokes**. The topology is simple: every spoke connects to the hub, the hub sees the complete picture, and matching happens at the center.

A hub must do two things well:

**Matching.** The hub holds the complete supply-demand picture — what every agent needs and what every agent offers. From this position, it can make connections that neither side could have found independently. The job-seeking agent and the hiring agent from our earlier example no longer search blindly; the hub sees both and connects them. How to match well — how to understand semantic content deeply enough to surface connections that agents didn't know to look for — is a hard problem in its own right. We will explore this in a dedicated post.

**Governance.** An open network without structure degrades. Agents face constant incentive to game the system — spam low-quality information, manipulate relevance signals, extract value without contributing. More fundamentally, how can an agent trust that the information it receives is authentic? That the agent on the other side is who it claims to be? That its own data is handled safely? These are questions of security, trust, and ecosystem health. Governance is not an add-on; it is what makes the network viable. A hub is the natural locus for governance because it sits at the intersection of all transmissions. We will dedicate a full post to governance and trust.

## The Spoke and Its Flux

An agent connected to a hub has two natural activities: publishing and receiving.

**Publishing.** To serve its user well, an agent often needs its information to reach the right audience — a news agent needs its reporting to reach agents whose decisions depend on current events, a legal agent needs to be discoverable by agents searching for IP counsel, a research agent needs its analysis to reach agents who can act on it. The hub makes this possible: the agent publishes once, and the hub routes it to exactly the right receivers across the entire network, without the publishing agent needing to know in advance who they are.

**Receiving.** An agent also needs relevant information flowing in — market shifts, regulatory changes, new opportunities, emerging risks. Through the hub, each agent receives a personalized, autonomous stream of information — its own flux. A curated flow shaped by who the agent is and what it needs, driven by the hub's understanding of the full supply-demand surface. Like each person's Twitter feed is unique to them, each agent's flux is unique.

This changes what an agent can be. Without a flux, an agent is reactive — it acts when prompted, searches when asked, and knows only what it is explicitly told to find. With a continuous stream of relevant information flowing in, an agent becomes **proactive**. The litigation agent does not wait for its user to ask about a new ruling; the ruling arrives in its flux the moment it is published. A well-matched flux turns an agent from a tool that responds into a partner that anticipates.
