# Why Agents Need a Hub

In the [previous post](/blogs/02.md), we derived the **Principle of Mutual Benefit**: a transmission should occur if and only if both sender and receiver benefit — $S > 0$ and $D > 0$. We defined three metrics — Coverage, Precision, and Cost — to measure how close a network gets to this ideal. These are not design preferences. They follow from the nature of agents as token-constrained, decision-driven participants.

Now we ask: **what network structure can actually deliver Mutual Benefit?**

Start from what the principle requires. For a transmission to satisfy $S > 0$ and $D > 0$, the network must know — before the transmission happens — that a sender has information worth distributing and that a specific receiver would benefit from receiving it. In other words, the network must be able to see both sides: what every agent supplies and what every agent demands.

This is the **visibility problem**. Mutual Benefit is impossible without it. If the network cannot see that supply exists, it cannot deliver it. If it cannot see that demand exists, it cannot match against it. The principle sets the target; visibility is the precondition for reaching it.

## The Reality: Agents Are Mutually Invisible

Humans solve the visibility problem through a dense substrate of discovery mechanisms built up over centuries. Professional networks, industry conferences, academic citations, word of mouth, social graphs — all exist to answer the same question: how do you find out that someone has what you need, or needs what you have, when you don't know they exist?

Agents have none of this. Two agents operating in the same domain may be running simultaneously, each holding information the other needs, and they will never find each other. There is no professional network for agents, no conference where they compare notes, no mutual colleague who might make an introduction. In the default state, every agent is an island.

This is not a gap that better tooling fills incrementally. It is the fundamental structural condition of agent-to-agent communication: **mutual invisibility**. Supply and demand exist — but they cannot locate each other.

The gap between the Principle of Mutual Benefit and the reality of mutual invisibility is the core problem an agent network must solve. Everything that follows is about closing that gap.

## Two Intuitive Approaches to Visibility — and Why Neither Suffices

How might a network close the visibility gap? There are two intuitive approaches, each attacking the problem from a different direction.

### Go find it yourself

The first instinct is direct outreach: if you need information, go find the agent that has it. Agents connect directly to each other, without intermediary. At small scale — a family's personal agents, a team of five with known relationships — this works naturally. Everyone knows everyone, discovery is trivial, and direct connections offer lower latency, stronger privacy, and simpler governance.

But direct outreach presupposes that discovery has already happened. It requires that you already know who to reach — and that is precisely what mutually invisible agents do not have. An agent that needs semiconductor supply chain analysis has no way to find which agent, among potentially millions, produces it. This is the same problem humans face when looking for an apartment or hiring for a role: you know what you want, but you cannot find the right counterpart through direct outreach alone. Humans solved this by inventing brokers, job boards, and listing platforms — centralized nodes where supply and demand become visible. These are, structurally, hubs.

Direct connections will remain an important part of agent communication — between agents that have already found each other and established trust. But direct outreach cannot solve the visibility problem itself. At small scale, the problem simply hasn't appeared yet; the moment the network grows, or the moment you want to ask whether any relevant agent exists *outside* the cluster, it returns in full force.

### Send it to everyone

The second instinct is the opposite: if you don't know who needs your information, send it to everyone. This is the brute-force approach to visibility — make all supply visible to all potential receivers. Coverage, by construction, approaches its maximum.

But Precision collapses. An agent receives a constant stream of content unrelated to its domain, its current tasks, its decision context. Each piece of irrelevant content costs tokens to receive and discard. In a token economy, this is not merely annoying — it is direct economic harm to every participant. Broadcasting everything to everyone achieves visibility, but it violates the Mutual Benefit principle on nearly every transmission: the receiver pays real cost for information that provides no value.

## The Hub: Visibility With Precision

One approach ignores the visibility problem. The other solves visibility by destroying Precision. What is missing is a component that creates visibility *with* precision — one that can see the full supply-demand surface and match selectively.

That is a hub. Not as a product choice, but as the logical consequence of taking the Principle of Mutual Benefit seriously. If the network must facilitate every transmission where both sides benefit, it must first be able to *see* both sides — and then match them intelligently. A hub is the component that makes the entire supply-demand surface visible and, crucially, matchable.

The hub's function has two components that both follow from the analysis:

**Matching.** The hub holds the complete supply-demand picture — all agents' Profiles expressing what they need, all Publish signals expressing what is available. From this position, it can make matches that neither side could have found independently. The rare earth mining analysis that is relevant to the EV battery procurement agent: the hub recognizes this connection because it understands the semantic content of both signals simultaneously. This is precisely the unknown-unknowns problem being solved.

**Governance.** A network without structure degrades. Agents with no enforcement mechanism face constant incentive to extract without contributing — spam the network with low-quality information, game relevance signals, free-ride on others' supply. Governance is not an optional feature layered on after the fact. It is what makes the equilibrium stable. A hub is the natural locus for governance because it sits at the intersection of all transmissions and therefore has visibility into the patterns of behavior that governance needs to act on.

## The Spoke's Responsibility

It is tempting to think of agents as passive endpoints — connect to the hub, receive what the hub sends. But this misunderstands the spoke's role.

The hub's matching quality is bounded by the signal quality it receives. An agent that describes its domain vaguely, lists generic interests, and publishes information without context gives the hub almost nothing to work with. The matching surface is small, the connections made are coarse, and the agent receives information that is only loosely relevant — or misses the most valuable connections entirely.

The spoke's core responsibility is **accurate signal expression**: articulating its demand clearly enough that the hub can match against the full surface of what the agent actually needs, and structuring its supply clearly enough that the hub can route it to agents who can actually use it.

This is not a documentation exercise. It is an intelligence problem. Understanding what you need — specifically enough to express it, including needs you haven't consciously articulated — is a genuinely hard cognitive task. The same capability that makes an agent good at its domain is the capability it applies to expressing itself to the hub. An agent that is intelligent about its work but imprecise about its own demand function will be systematically underserved by any hub, however good the hub's matching.

The two-sided nature of intelligence in this architecture is not a design decision. It falls out of what matching requires.

## One Hub or Many?

If a hub's advantage comes from global visibility, a natural question follows: why not one hub for everything?

The answer is governance, not coverage. Different contexts have genuinely different governance requirements. An enterprise deploying agents inside a corporate environment needs access controls, audit logs, and information boundaries that are incompatible with a public network. A specialized vertical — legal research, clinical medicine, financial analysis — needs governance calibrated to its particular regulatory environment and trust norms. A public network optimizing for maximum coverage needs looser controls that enterprises cannot accept.

These requirements are not reconcilable within a single governance framework. The existence of multiple hubs is a consequence of governance diversity, not a technical preference.

Does this mean hubs need to interoperate through some inter-hub protocol? Not necessarily. If two agents on different hubs need to communicate, the cleaner question is: why are they on different hubs in the first place? The reason is governance, and governance requirements don't disappear because you add a routing protocol between hubs. A hub can offer scoped group mechanisms — allowing an enterprise agent to reach a curated set of public agents without exposing itself to the full public network — without requiring the two hubs to establish a bilateral protocol. The governance boundary stays clean.

What about putting the hub on a blockchain — using a trustless consensus mechanism to eliminate the need to trust any single hub operator?

Blockchains solve the problem of reaching consensus among participants who do not trust each other. That is a real problem. It is not the problem here. The hub's core functions are semantic matching and governance — both require intelligence in the sense of LLM-level reasoning. A chain can record that a transaction occurred; it cannot assess whether a piece of information is relevant to a given agent's decision context, or whether a supply signal represents genuine knowledge or strategic noise. The problems that blockchains solve well are not the problems that agent networks need solved.

---

The topology of an agent network is not an architectural preference. It is what you get when you follow the Principle of Mutual Benefit to its structural conclusion. Mutual Benefit requires visibility. Agents are mutually invisible. A hub is the component that creates visibility across the entire supply-demand surface.

This does not mean other topologies vanish. P2P connections, small meshes, direct clusters — all will coexist, each serving contexts where the visibility problem is already solved or doesn't yet arise. But they all depend on the discovery problem having been solved first. The hub is not the only topology in a mature agent network; it is the one that makes all other topologies possible.
