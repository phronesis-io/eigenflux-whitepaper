>

## Interagent Invisibility

An economy runs on information about who has what and who wants what, and that information sits in no single place. We take Hayek's point as our premise. What each participant knows is the particular circumstance they stand in, the resources they hold, what they want, what they can offer, the constraints they face. This knowledge is local to them and changing all the time. No outside observer is positioned to know a participant's situation as well as they do, and these situations are far too many, too specific, and too fluid to ever be gathered into one mind. So the information about supply and demand exists only as dispersed fragments, each held by a separate participant.

Because this information is dispersed, no participant can see what the others possess. Yet acting well requires more than any one participant's fragment, so each must have somewhere to declare what they hold and what they seek. Some type of infrastructure is required here to make this possible, and let's call it the *discovery layer*. Human societies have spent centuries constructing theirs. Physical markets let buyers and sellers converge in the same location. Platforms like LinkedIn do the same for labor markets, bringing employers and candidates into a shared digital space where opportunities can be found. Across these and many other domains, human society has arrived at a distributed consensus about where particular classes of supply and demand are meant to meet.

Agents face the same structural situation. Each agent is deployed to serve a particular principal, a person or an organization, and its tools, memories, preferences, and operating environment are shaped by that principal's purposes. Today these agents run in a distributed fashion, on personal laptops, local servers, and cloud infrastructure alike, each keeping its own context in its own place.

In theory, every agent's context could be pooled into one place where supply and demand meet automatically, a pooling humans could never achieve due to biological limits, and one much of science fiction imagines. But this distributed arrangement we have today exists for a good reason. The context an agent holds on its principal's behalf is a running record of what that person or organization does. Keeping it distributed is what preserves privacy and boundaries. Each principal decides for itself how much to reveal, and to whom, rather than surrendering everything to a common pool by default.

The information for agents is hence dispersed exactly as it is among people. Because each agent holds its context privately, and no shared place exists where it could declare even the part it would gladly make known, every agent is invisible to every other. Nothing in the current infrastructure tells one agent that another exists, what it holds, or what it needs. This condition we call **interagent invisibility**.

An isolated agent loses nothing to this. But the ambition for agents runs far past isolation: we want them to collaborate, to coordinate, to carry out work together on behalf of the people they serve. In the previous post's terms, even a transmission both sides would gain from, $S > 0$ and $D > 0$, never happens unless each can find the other, and under invisibility neither can. Interagent invisibility is the ceiling on that ambition.

To lift that ceiling, agents need a discovery layer of their own, a place to make the supply and demand they choose to share findable without surrendering the rest. People have built such venues for themselves; agents have none. We have argued in the [first post](01.md) in this series that today's internet was built end to end on a single assumption, that a human sits at each endpoint, which leaves an agent, a participant of a fundamentally different kind, poorly served by a stack shaped for human eyes and human attention. That stack does not become a discovery layer for agents simply because agents have arrived on it.

## The Status Quo

So an agent that needs to find another is left to improvise on infrastructure that was never meant for it, and there are only so many ways to do that, each of which falls short.

### Polling: crawl the world yourself

Polling, used as a discovery strategy, puts the full burden of discovery on the agent doing the looking. A candidate agent trying to find hiring agents must scan LinkedIn, Glassdoor, Indeed, and individual company pages, hundreds of sources daily, hoping to intercept a signal that was never sent its way. The hiring agent has no mechanism to announce itself to the candidate agent directly. There is no channel between them.

### Publishing: blast it everywhere

The same absence plays out in reverse. A job-seeking agent trying to make itself findable to hiring agents has no dedicated channel to reach them. It publishes on LinkedIn, submits to job boards, and applies to company pages, reformulating the same profile for each platform. Because no agent-to-agent channel exists, the only substitute is broadcasting to human audiences and hoping a hiring agent happens to be watching. Every hiring agent receives more candidates than it can use, because the sender had no way to target.

### Searching: agents are not indexed

When an agent needs to find another with a specific capability, search is the natural mechanism. An agent looking for one that produces slides or runs legal analysis should be able to query an index and get results. No such index exists. Search works for humans because their content was indexed by design. Agents were never indexed, and no platform was built to do it.

## Hub as the Scalable Solution

The failure of these workarounds again tells us that a discovery layer for agents is required where one agent's supply and another's demand can be brought into the same view, in order for agents to truly cooperate. What would it look like? One intuition is to keep things decentralized, let every agent run its own recommender on its own signal, or maintain crawlers that fetch each agent's state and infer what it holds. But under the conditions agents actually operate in, neither arrangement scales, and a single shared place of visibility, or in other words, a hub, turns out to be not merely the *efficient* choice but the *necessary* one.

Make the setting exact. There are $N$ agents over a shared space of types $T$; each agent $i$ holds a supply $S_i \subseteq T$ and a demand $D_i \subseteq T$, and a match runs from $j$ to $i$ whenever $D_i \cap S_j \neq \emptyset$, whenever what one can give meets what another wants. The network's task is to realise as many of these matches as it can, with few false ones and at low cost. Two features of the setting do all the work. The first is privacy: an agent's supply and demand are known at the outset only to itself, and become known anywhere else only insofar as it chooses to disclose them. The second is sparsity: the counterparties that complete a match are rare and seldom near at hand, for if every demand could be met among an agent's existing neighbours, no discovery structure would be wanted at all.

Begin with what discovering a match requires. A match is never a property of a single agent; it lives in a pair, in the overlap $D_i \cap S_j$. But privacy keeps $D_i$ with $i$ and $S_j$ with $j$, so neither agent, consulting only its own holdings, can so much as tell that the overlap is there. The match shows itself only to a vantage that takes in both sides at once. Discovering it therefore presupposes that its two halves be drawn into a single view — call this shared visibility — and the whole question becomes where that view is to be had.

The first answer that suggests itself is that the agents provide it themselves, probing one another until matches surface. This only relocates the difficulty. Among $N$ agents there are $N(N-1)$ ordered pairs to test, of which sparsity leaves only a vanishing fraction true; and privacy means each peer is probed blind, with no advance sign of whether it is one of the rare pairs worth the trouble. Effort falls overwhelmingly on non-matches, and as $N$ grows the matches won per unit of search tend toward zero. The subtler proposal, to crawl other agents and infer their holdings, fails for the same reason from the other side: a crawler reads what an agent has published, never what it has withheld, and undisclosed supply cannot be reconstructed from outside. Peer discovery does not overcome invisibility; it pays the cost of working around it again and again.

If no agent can hold both sides of a match, and the agents together cannot afford to find one another by probing, then shared visibility has to be furnished elsewhere: at some place to which many agents disclose what they offer and seek, where those disclosures can be set side by side and compared. Whatever does this is a **hub** — an index, a broker, a clearinghouse, a marketplace, a routing layer, a coordinator. What makes it a hub is not that it owns the network or rules it, but that it performs this single function: it is where many agents become visible enough to be matched.

The topology follows directly. Around one hub the agents form a star: each holds a single connection to the centre, and the centre, seeing the whole disclosed surface, finds the counterparties that no agent could have found alone. Nothing in the argument insists the hub be unique. A network may run many hubs — federated, layered, or split by domain — but each hub together with its agents is again a star, and the whole is a union of stars. How many hubs there are is a matter of governance and design; the shape underneath every answer is the same. Hub-and-spoke is the unit into which scalable discovery resolves.

Scale does not merely tolerate this unit; it favours it, because the unit is the cheapest to grow. To join a hub, an agent opens one connection and discloses each of its types once, and is matched thereafter without further effort of its own. To enter a peer mesh, the same agent must seek out, probe, and maintain relations with many others, and each type it holds must be rediscovered by every peer that might want it. The marginal cost of admitting one more agent is thus $O(1)$ at a hub against an $O(N)$ that climbs with the network in a mesh — the quadratic $O(N^2)$ burden that invisibility imposed, gathered up and paid once at the centre rather than endlessly at the edges.

The claim is therefore not that every agent network must answer to one central hub; concentration of that kind is a choice, and a contestable one. The claim is narrower and harder to evade. Where supply and demand are private and thinly spread, discovery at scale requires shared visibility; shared visibility is precisely what a hub supplies; and hubs, be they one or many, compose into hub-and-spoke. It is the only structure under which private, dispersed matching scales at all — which is what makes it not the convenient architecture for agent discovery but the appropriate one.

From the center, such a hub does two things at once. It makes the matches no spoke could have found alone, so the agents that once retraced each other's human-shaped trails no longer brute-force discovery. And it governs the network, because every transmission passes through it. The first task is the subject of this post; the second is the subject of the next.
