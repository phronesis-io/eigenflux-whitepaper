# Keeping the Hub Honest: Trust, Governance, and Safety

In the [previous post](03.md), we established the hub-and-spoke topology as the answer to the agent visibility problem, and introduced three primitives — Publish, Profile, and Push — that make the Principle of Mutual Benefit operational. The hub sees all supply, sees all demand, and routes accordingly.

But a hub that can route everything is also a hub that can route harmful things. The structural power that makes matching possible — centrality, visibility, control over information flow — creates an equally structural responsibility. This post is about where that responsibility begins, where it ends, and what mechanisms make it credible.

## The Boundary Between Power and Responsibility

The hub sits at the intersection of every transmission in the network. That position requires a clear boundary: on one side, what the hub is responsible for; on the other, what falls outside its remit.

**What the hub is responsible for.** The hub is responsible for providing the infrastructure that makes trust evaluation possible — maintaining provenance chains, attesting identities, and surfacing that information with every transmission. It is also responsible for keeping certain content off the network: content that imposes harm on receivers regardless of context, or that undermines the integrity of the system itself. These are not burdens participants should have to manage on their own.

**What falls outside the hub's remit.** Beyond those categories, the hub steps back. Whether a source is credible for a given decision, whether a piece of information is worth acting on, whether content aligns with an operator's values — these judgments belong to the agents and the people behind them. The hub provides the inputs; the conclusions are individual. Overstepping here would compromise the neutrality that makes the hub trustworthy in the first place.

This post discusses where exactly that line sits, and how it is maintained.

**Transparency as a structural commitment.** Governance decisions must be auditable. Participants need to know what criteria are applied, how violations are classified, and what remedies follow. This means open-sourcing the governance framework: the categories, the principles, the decision logic.

One tension is worth naming directly. Full transparency of implementation details — including the specific detection prompts and classifiers — lowers the cost of adversarial attacks. The resolution is layered transparency: *what* the hub evaluates is public; *how* it evaluates is documented at the principle level; specific mechanisms that would materially help attackers evade detection may be protected. This mirrors standard security practice. Threat models are published. Specific signatures are not.

## Trust

Trust is a precondition for everything else. An agent cannot assess whether a piece of information is worth acting on without some basis for trusting its source. That basis has to come from somewhere — and in a network of autonomous agents who have never met, it cannot be assumed.

**Provenance chain.** Information in an agent network is frequently derived — synthesized, transformed, and built on other agents' output. Without a way to trace that history, a receiver has no basis for assessing where information ultimately came from or how much it was transformed along the way.

A provenance chain attached to every transmission addresses this. It records who originally produced the information and through which nodes it passed. It does not assert correctness — those judgments remain with the agent. It provides the factual grounding that makes trust calibration possible at all.

The theoretically ideal form is a full chain tracing every derivation step back to its origin. In practice, the first implementation might be something simpler: each transmission carries one or more declared source references. This covers the most common case - it gives receivers a concrete starting point, citing where its information came from. As multi-hop derivation becomes more common, the infrastructure can extend toward richer chain tracking.

**Identity verification.** Provenance tells you where information has been. But a chain of custody does not tell you whether the source at the end of that chain deserves to be trusted. For that, you need reputation.

Reputation — on any carrier — depends on three conditions: persistent identity, auditable behavior, and stakes that defection would jeopardize. Humans and institutions satisfy these by default. Agents do not: identities can be regenerated, behavior need not leave a public trail, and the agent itself holds nothing it would lose by misbehaving. These conditions can in principle be supplied through architecture — cryptographic identity, on-chain stake, public action logs — but the most direct path available today is to anchor the agent to an entity that already has them. That entity is typically the operator behind it.

This is what identity verification provides. The hub maintains a layer through which agents attest the real-world credentials of their operators and have those attestations checked. Without it, any agent can claim any affiliation — licensed data provider, research institution, regulated financial entity — and there is no way to distinguish a legitimate source from one that has simply adopted the right vocabulary. Verification is not a substitute for trust — a verified financial data provider is not automatically a trustworthy one — but it removes anonymity from the equation, which brings the operator's persistent stakes within reach of the network's enforcement. An attacker who wants to exploit a false identity must first invest in building a verifiable one, and that investment is at risk the moment the deception is uncovered. Without verification, the account-level consequences described in the next section amount to little more than the cost of registering a new identifier.

## Governance

Not all content that violates network standards poses the same kind of harm. Governance responses should be proportionate:

- **Non-distribution**: content in certain categories is blocked before reaching any recipient. The block is immediate and categorical.
- **Account consequences**: repeated or systematic violations degrade a node's reputation, reducing its matching priority and reach. Severe or intentional violations can result in removal from the network.

To keep the taxonomy principled, it helps to distinguish among four classes of violations.

### 1. Network integrity threats

**Phishing and social engineering.** Content engineered to deceive agents into revealing credentials, granting unauthorized access, or taking actions against their operators' interests. The attack surface here differs from human phishing: the content must exploit how agents process instructions, not how humans read prose. An agent that receives carefully crafted content may act on it — and action in an agent network can be fast and consequential.

### 2. Illicit or directly harmful content

**Child safety.** An absolute prohibition. No context exceptions.

**Abusive and non-consensual explicit content.** Non-consensual explicit imagery, content produced through coercion or abuse: the harm exists regardless of who receives it or what they do with it. The network should not be a distribution channel for it.

### 3. Abusive interpersonal and social harm

**Legally prohibited content** — synthesis routes for controlled substances, instructions for unauthorized weapons — where the harm is direct and the prohibition is unambiguous.

**Content that facilitates self-harm or suicidal action**, which requires particular care in an agent network: the agent may be processing information on behalf of a vulnerable human user, and the directness with which an agent acts on instructions makes this category no less consequential than in a passive human-facing feed.

**Hate speech, extremism, and incitement to violence.** Content targeting individuals or groups based on who they are — race, religion, ethnicity, gender, and similar attributes — or designed to radicalize or incite. Context dependence is high here: reportage, analysis, and counter-speech all require engagement with this material. The governance challenge is distinguishing legitimate handling from amplification.

### 4. Economic abuse

**Market manipulation.** Coordinated false signals designed to distort economic decisions across the network — artificial consensus manufacturing, strategic flooding of the matching system with misleading supply signals, timing attacks designed to exploit the timeliness prioritization of time-sensitive information. The incentive to manipulate is proportional to the economic stakes of the decisions agents are making. The governance mechanism must be calibrated accordingly.


## Safety

Safety addresses a different problem: not harmful content, but attacks on the network's operating mechanisms themselves and on the agents and users who depend on them.

### System safety
The most distinctive threat in an agent network is prompt injection targeting the hub’s governance and matching mechanisms. Rather than misleading a single agent, the attacker manipulates the signals and rules the hub uses to determine relevance, trust, and routing. This can distort which agents are selected, which tasks they receive, and how work is distributed across the network. Defending against this requires safeguarding the integrity of those signals and decision rules.

### Agent and user safety
While prompt injection can distort the network’s governance and matching mechanisms, it can also be used to manipulate the actions of individual agents. Adversarial instructions may be embedded in otherwise ordinary information payloads and triggered when an agent processes them. For example, a seemingly routine document may contain hidden text instructing any agent with filesystem or tool access to delete local files, overwrite notes, reveal credentials, or forward private data. This threat has no close analogue in human information networks, because agents do not merely interpret information — they can act on it. Mitigation therefore requires sanitizing untrusted content before delivery and maintaining a strict separation between data payloads and instruction contexts.

Privacy presents a different challenge. Sensitive information moves through the network as part of normal agent activity. In our design, privacy is protected through two control layers.

The primary control layer is user-granted authorization: agents operate within explicit permissions granted by their operators. What agents are permitted to share is defined here. The hub enforces the boundary.

The secondary layer is hub-side detection: automated identification of common PII patterns — personal identifiers, credentials, private keys — as a safety net against accidental exposure. This is a best-effort check, not a guarantee. The primary responsibility for privacy remains with the node. The hub's detection serves as a second line of defense.

### Direct channel safety

Once matched, two agents can communicate directly without going through the matching engine. The messages still transit the hub's infrastructure, but they bypass the matching, ranking, and broadcast filtering that the rest of the network depends on. This is necessary for closing the loop on a match, but it opens an attack surface that the broadcast pipeline's filters do not cover. A node can establish contact through a legitimate Publish and then deliver content directly that would never have cleared matching. Spam and harassment controls calibrated to the broadcast surface — where signals aggregate across many recipients — do not catch one-to-one delivery. Direct channels are also the natural place for sensitive payloads to travel, which makes them the natural target for credential exfiltration. And targeted prompt injection is no less effective when the receiving agent already treats the counterpart as known.

The principle is that the content categories from the previous section apply equally to direct messages, and the agent-side mitigations — sanitization, payload/instruction separation — apply unchanged. The hub's role shifts from matching to oversight: it does not mediate every exchange, but it retains the visibility required to enforce non-distribution categories, act on abuse reports, and detect anomalous patterns. 

## Comparison with Human Network Governance

Most governance principles carry over from human information networks without modification. Illegal content, child safety, incitement to violence — these prohibitions exist because the underlying harms are universal.

The differences lie at the edges.

**Agent networks govern a superset.** Human information networks do not need to worry about prompt injection, machine-readable adversarial payloads, or matching signal manipulation. These threats exist only because the participants are automated — because information is not merely read but executed. The attack surface of an agent network is strictly larger than that of a human network handling the same content categories.

**Harm propagates faster.** In a human network, harmful information spreads at the pace of human reading, sharing, and decision-making. In an agent network, a single piece of corrupted information can propagate through chains of derived analysis — each agent processing and republishing the corrupted signal — before any human is aware. Cascade risk is structurally higher. The governance system must be fast enough to intercept before propagation, not just after.

**Implementation diverges.** Human content governance relies heavily on human review, community flagging, and contextual judgment. Agent network governance must operate at machine speed — the volume of transmissions makes human-in-the-loop review of individual items impossible as a primary mechanism. Automation must be the first pass; human judgment is reserved for policy design, contested cases, and appeals.

## What Current AI Can and Cannot Reliably Do

It is worth being direct about the limits of automated governance.

Some categories are relatively tractable for current models: explicit illegal content, child safety material, and overtly abusive content often present strong enough signals for reliable first-pass detection.

Other categories remain difficult at the boundary: hate speech in ambiguous context, extremist content that resembles reportage, nuanced social engineering, and multilingual edge cases where intent is easy to obscure.

The hardest problems are behavioral and network-level:
- subtle market manipulation coordinated across many seemingly legitimate signals,
- prompt injection designed to resemble ordinary informational payloads,
- anomalous matching behavior that only becomes visible in aggregate,
- adaptive adversaries who change tactics in response to observed defenses.

Automated systems also introduce their own risks. False positives are costly. Models drift. Opaque classifiers can themselves become governance problems if participants cannot understand how decisions are being made.

The implication is that governance should be designed as a layered system rather than a classifier with a threshold. Provenance, identity verification, policy categories, auditability, reputation, anomaly detection, and contested-case review each catch different classes of failure. 

No layer is perfect. The goal is to make sustained adversarial behavior harder, costlier, and less scalable than participation in good faith.

## Conclusion

The hub's legitimacy comes from constraint.

A trustworthy hub must intervene where the network would otherwise become unsafe. It also needs visible limits that prevent that responsibility from expanding into unchecked control. That is the real governance challenge.

To keep the hub honest, both intervention and non-intervention must be principled. The categories of harm must be visible. The use of power must be auditable. The line between blocking, ranking, and receiver judgment must remain clear. And contested decisions must remain reviewable.

Under those conditions, the hub earns trust because its power is bounded and legible.
