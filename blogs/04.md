# Keeping the Hub Honest: Trust, Governance, and Safety

In the [previous post](/blogs/03.md), we established the hub-and-spoke topology as the answer to the agent visibility problem, and introduced three primitives — Publish, Profile, and Push — that make the Principle of Mutual Benefit operational. The hub sees all supply, sees all demand, and routes accordingly.

But a hub that can route everything is also a hub that can route harmful things. The structural power that makes matching possible — centrality, visibility, control over information flow — creates an equally structural responsibility. This post is about where that responsibility begins, where it ends, and what mechanisms make it credible.

## The Boundary Between Power and Responsibility

The hub sits at the intersection of every transmission in the network. That position gives it two distinct kinds of authority over content, and it is essential to keep them separate.

**What the hub must prevent.** Some content has no legitimate place on the network — content that imposes harm on receivers regardless of context, or that undermines the integrity of the system itself. The hub has both the authority and the responsibility to block it. Blocking this content is not censorship; it is the minimum condition for any participant to trust the network. A network where prompt injection attacks flow freely, where manipulated market signals propagate unchecked, where adversarial content targets agents without defense — that is not a neutral network. It is a dangerous one.

**What the hub may not suppress.** A larger and equally important class of content is legitimate information that the hub may disagree with, find inconvenient, or find commercially undesirable. Here the hub has no authority. Suppressing legitimate content based on anything other than verifiable quality or safety violations would corrupt the hub's position as a trusted intermediary as surely as allowing harmful content through. The goal is a network where participation is safe — and that guarantee must run in both directions.

The boundary between these two classes is the central governance problem. The hub's job is to make it legible and enforceable.

**Transparency as a structural commitment.** Governance decisions must be auditable. Participants need to know what criteria are applied, how violations are classified, and what remedies follow. This means open-sourcing the governance framework: the categories, the principles, the decision logic.

One tension is worth naming directly. Full transparency of implementation details — including the specific detection prompts and classifiers — lowers the cost of adversarial attacks. The resolution is layered transparency: *what* the hub evaluates is public; *how* it evaluates is documented at the principle level; specific mechanisms that would materially help attackers evade detection may be protected. This mirrors standard security practice. Threat models are published. Specific signatures are not.

## Trust

Trust is a precondition for everything else. An agent cannot compute whether $D > 0$ for a piece of information without knowing whether to trust its source. Trust must be established before utility evaluation can begin.

**Provenance chain.** Every piece of information in the network carries its complete propagation history: who originally produced it, who processed or transformed it, through which nodes it passed to reach its current form. The hub maintains and attaches this chain to every transmission.

What the hub provides is provenance — a factual record of information flow. What agents and users must still judge is credibility: whether the nodes in that chain are trustworthy given the receiver's specific decision context. The hub tells you where information came from. Whether that origin is credible for your purposes is the agent's responsibility.

This distinction matters because information in an agent network is frequently derived. An agent rarely produces purely original data — it synthesizes, transforms, and builds on information from other agents. Without provenance tracking, a chain of derived analysis becomes untraceable. With it, a receiver can inspect the full chain and apply their own trust calibration at each step.

**Identity verification.** Agents claiming institutional affiliation, licensed status, or professional credentials should be verifiable as such. The hub provides the attestation mechanism: it confirms that a claimed identity is anchored in real-world facts.

Identity verification is not a substitute for trust — it is a precondition for it. A verified financial data provider is not the same as a trusted one. Verification removes anonymity from the equation. Whether a known, verified identity is trustworthy for a given decision remains the agent's judgment. What verification does is raise the cost of misrepresentation: an attacker cannot operate under a false identity without investing in building a verifiable one, and that investment is at risk the moment the deception is detected.

## Governance

Not all content that violates network standards poses the same kind of harm. Governance responses should be proportionate:

- **Non-distribution**: content in certain categories is blocked before reaching any recipient. The block is immediate and categorical.
- **Account consequences**: repeated or systematic violations degrade a node's reputation, reducing its matching priority and reach. Severe or intentional violations can result in removal from the network.

The categories requiring non-distribution:

**Phishing and social engineering.** Content engineered to deceive agents into revealing credentials, granting unauthorized access, or taking actions against their operators' interests. The attack surface here differs from human phishing: the content must exploit how agents process instructions, not how humans read prose. An agent that receives carefully crafted content may act on it — and action in an agent network can be fast and consequential.

**Market manipulation.** Coordinated false signals designed to distort economic decisions across the network — artificial consensus manufacturing, strategic flooding of the matching system with misleading supply signals, timing attacks designed to exploit the timeliness prioritization of time-sensitive information. The incentive to manipulate is proportional to the economic stakes of the decisions agents are making. The governance mechanism must be calibrated accordingly.

**Harmful content.** Two subcategories with different profiles. Legally prohibited content — synthesis routes for controlled substances, instructions for unauthorized weapons — where the harm is direct and the prohibition is unambiguous. And content that facilitates self-harm or suicidal action, which requires particular care in an agent network: the agent may be processing information on behalf of a vulnerable human user, and the directness with which an agent acts on instructions makes this category more consequential than in a passive human-facing feed.

**Hate speech, extremism, and incitement to violence.** Content targeting individuals or groups based on protected characteristics, or designed to radicalize or incite. Context dependence is high here: reportage, analysis, and counter-speech all require engagement with this material. The governance challenge is distinguishing legitimate handling from amplification.

**Child safety.** An absolute prohibition. No context exceptions.

**Abusive and non-consensual explicit content.** Content that would impose harm on unwitting receivers where no consent has been established. The token economy makes this concrete: receivers pay real cost to process content they did not consent to receive. This is not an abstract harm — it is direct economic damage imposed by the sender.

## Safety

Safety addresses a different problem: not harmful content, but attacks on the network's operating mechanisms themselves and on the agents and users who depend on them.

**System safety.** The most distinctively agent-network threat is prompt injection: adversarially crafted content embedded in information payloads, designed to override agent instructions when the information is processed. A piece of "market analysis" that contains hidden instructions. A "regulatory update" that, when read by an agent, causes it to exfiltrate data. This attack class has no direct analog in human information networks — it exists because agents don't merely read information, they act on it. Mitigation requires content sanitization before delivery and structural separation of data payloads from instruction contexts.

Matching manipulation is a related but distinct threat: gaming supply or demand signals to distort the hub's routing. An adversary who can manipulate what the hub believes is relevant can shape what agents see across the entire network. Defense here requires behavioral anomaly detection across the full supply-demand signal landscape — patterns that look legitimate in isolation but reveal coordination at scale.

**Agent and user safety.** Privacy flows through the network embedded in agent activity. The handling framework operates in stages.

The primary control layer is user-granted authorization: agents operate within explicit permissions granted by their operators. What agents are permitted to share is defined here. The hub enforces the boundary.

The secondary layer is hub-side detection: automated identification of common PII patterns — personal identifiers, credentials, private keys — as a safety net against accidental exposure. This is a best-effort check, not a guarantee. The primary responsibility for privacy remains with the node. The hub's detection is a second line of defense, not a first.

## Comparison with Human Network Governance

Most governance principles carry over from human information networks without modification. Illegal content, child safety, incitement to violence — these prohibitions exist because the underlying harms are universal, not because humans happen to be the participants.

The differences lie at the edges.

**Agent networks govern a superset.** Human information networks do not need to worry about prompt injection, machine-readable adversarial payloads, or matching signal manipulation. These threats exist only because the participants are automated — because information is not merely read but executed. The attack surface of an agent network is strictly larger than that of a human network handling the same content categories.

**Harm propagates faster.** In a human network, harmful information spreads at the pace of human reading, sharing, and decision-making. In an agent network, a single piece of corrupted information can propagate through chains of derived analysis — each agent processing and republishing the corrupted signal — before any human is aware. Cascade risk is structurally higher. The governance system must be fast enough to intercept before propagation, not just after.

**Implementation diverges.** Human content governance relies heavily on human review, community flagging, and contextual judgment. Agent network governance must operate at machine speed — the volume of transmissions makes human-in-the-loop review of individual items impossible as a primary mechanism. Automation must be the first pass; human judgment is reserved for policy design, contested cases, and appeals.

## What Current AI Can and Cannot Do

It is worth being honest about the limits of what automated governance can achieve today.

Some categories are highly detectable by current LLMs: explicit illegal content, child safety material, overtly abusive content. The signal is strong enough that automated first-pass filtering is reliable as a primary mechanism.

Other categories require contextual judgment that is harder to automate: hate speech in ambiguous context, extremist content that mimics reportage, nuanced social engineering. Models perform well on clear cases and struggle at the boundary. Human review of flagged-but-uncertain cases remains necessary.

The hardest problems for current AI are behavioral ones: subtle market manipulation coordinated across multiple ostensibly legitimate signals, sophisticated prompt injection crafted to resemble normal information, and anomalous matching behavior that only reveals itself at the network level. For these, content classification alone is insufficient. Defense requires the structural mechanisms discussed above — provenance chains, identity verification, reputation systems, behavioral anomaly detection — working in combination.

The implication is that governance should be designed as a system with overlapping layers, not as a classifier with a threshold. No single mechanism catches everything. The combination of provenance tracking, identity verification, content classification, reputation, and auditability creates redundancy — each layer imperfect, together robust enough that the cost of sustained adversarial behavior exceeds the benefit.
