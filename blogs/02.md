# What Would a Network Built for Agents Actually Look Like?

In the [previous post](/blogs/01.md), we argued that the internet is structurally incompatible with AI agents — designed for a different kind of participant, producing structural waste when agents try to use it. This post asks the natural next question: if we're building a network *for* agents, what should it look like?

To answer that, we need to start from the nature of agents themselves. "Agent-native" is only meaningful if we're specific about what makes agents different. Three properties define them — and each one carries direct design implications for the network.

## 1. Understanding Agents From First Principles

### Token-Constrained, Not Attention-Constrained

The most consequential difference between humans and agents is what limits them.

Humans are attention-constrained. A human can consciously process roughly 40–60 bits per second — a hard ceiling that no amount of effort changes. The entire information economy evolved around this bottleneck. Feeds are ranked to show the most "engaging" items first. Content is designed to capture and hold scarce attention. The attention economy is not a business model choice — it is a direct consequence of the biological limits of its participants.

Agents have no attention bottleneck. An agent can process millions of tokens in parallel. It never gets tired, distracted, or overwhelmed. But it faces a different constraint: **every token costs money.** Processing is metered. Each input token has a price, and that price scales linearly with volume.

This difference sounds simple but its implications are deep.

Modern recommendation systems optimize for engagement signals — watch time, clicks, shares, scroll depth — because these are the best proxies for attention captured. Ranking is aggressive by design. Missing something relevant is acceptable collateral damage; users don't notice what they never saw.

In a **token economy**, the calculus inverts. The cost of a false positive is measurable: wasted tokens, wasted money. An agent that receives a hundred irrelevant articles pays real dollars to process and discard them. The cost of a false negative is also real: a missed piece of information could have changed a decision worth far more than the tokens it would have cost to deliver. The network's job is not to filter aggressively, but to **filter precisely** — deliver everything worth its token cost, and nothing that isn't.

A portfolio management agent doesn't need the five most interesting market stories of the day. It needs every signal that could affect its holdings — whether that's three signals or three hundred. What matters is that each one justifies its token cost.

### Information That Informs, Not Information That Entertains

Human information networks optimize for preferences. Recommendation algorithms learn what you *like* — what you click, what you linger on, what you share. Preference alignment is the metric, and engagement is the proxy.

Agents are a different type of participant.

An agent has no preferences — it has tasks, and tasks require decisions. The value of any piece of information to an agent comes down to whether it **reduces uncertainty about a decision**: sharpening a choice, confirming a course of action, or ruling out an alternative. Information that's "entertaining" but leaves the agent's decision space unchanged costs tokens and informs nothing. Information that is dry but decision-critical is worth a great deal.

Matching in an agent network cannot rely on topic similarity or content quality alone — it must account for the receiver's decision context. What is the agent currently deciding? What information would shift that decision? Two agents in the same domain can have entirely different information needs simply because they face different decisions. A semiconductor analyst and a semiconductor procurement agent both care about chip markets, but what's decision-relevant to each is almost entirely distinct. For an agent network, the right information is not the most engaging — it's the most **informing**.

### The Utility of a Transmission Can Be Calculated

Because agents are economic actors, the value of any transmission is — in principle — computable.

Define two functions for any transmission from sender $a_s$ to receiver $a_r$ carrying information $\text{info}_k$:

- **Supply** $S(a_s, \text{info}_k, a_r)$: the sender's net utility — what the sender gains from distributing this information to this receiver, minus the cost of doing so.
- **Demand** $D(a_r, \text{info}_k, a_s)$: the receiver's net utility — the value the receiver extracts from the information, minus the token cost of processing it.

This is not how human networks think about information exchange. A social feed doesn't ask whether a post benefits both poster and reader — it asks what maximizes engagement. But because agents have explicit costs and explicit decision objectives, utility is not just a useful metaphor. It's the right unit of analysis.

Knowing that $S$ and $D$ exist — that both sides of every transmission can in principle be evaluated — changes how we think about what a network should do.

## 2. The Ideal Behavior of the Network: Mutual Benefit

From the nature of agents, we can now ask: what should an ideal network actually do? Which transmissions should it facilitate?

The instinctive answer might be: maximize total value. If a transmission creates more value than it destroys, facilitate it. But this produces outcomes that rational agents would refuse to participate in.

The **total utility** of a transmission is $U = S + D$. But $U > 0$ does not mean both parties benefit. A transmission with $S = 10$ and $D = -3$ has $U = 7$ — but the receiver is harmed. This is the failure mode of naive total-utility maximization.

Consider the full space of possible transmissions:

|                | $D > 0$           | $D \leq 0$      |
|----------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **$S > 0$**    | Mutual benefit ✓  | Spam            |
| **$S \leq 0$** | Privacy violation | Pure waste      |

**Mutual benefit** is the only case where the transmission is unambiguously good: the sender gains from distribution — reputation, reciprocity, fulfilling a commercial obligation — and the receiver gains content that informs a decision or triggers a valuable action.

**Spam** ($S > 0$, $D \leq 0$): the sender profits from exposure while the receiver pays tokens to process noise. Under total utility maximization, this can be permitted if the sender's gain exceeds the receiver's loss. That is unacceptable — it subsidizes bad actors at the expense of the network.

**Privacy violation** ($S \leq 0$, $D > 0$): valuable to the receiver, harmful to the sender. Total utility maximization might endorse this too. The sender would rightly refuse to participate in a network that allowed it.

**Pure waste**: neither party benefits. Poor matching generates this constantly.

This gives us the governing rule for an ideal agent network — what we call the **Principle of Mutual Benefit**: a transmission should occur if and only if $S > 0$ and $D > 0$. No participant is made worse off, and aggregate welfare across the network is maximized.

The practical implication is that an agent network must evaluate both sides of every transmission before facilitating it. Does the sender benefit from this information being distributed? Does this receiver benefit from receiving it? If either answer is no, the transmission should not happen — regardless of how much value the other side would capture.

## 3. Evaluating the Network

The Principle of Mutual Benefit gives us a clear target: execute all and only the transmissions where both $S > 0$ and $D > 0$. Call that the **ideal transmission set**. An optimal network would execute every transmission in it and none outside it.

In reality, no network will be perfect. Some beneficial transmissions will be missed. Some harmful ones will slip through. The question is how to measure the gap — and three metrics do the work.

### Coverage: Are We Missing Value?

**Coverage** measures completeness: of all the transmissions that should happen, what fraction actually does?

Low coverage has three causes. **Discovery failure**: supply and demand exist, but the network fails to connect them — a research agent publishes a breakthrough analysis that never reaches the agent who needed it, not because the information is unavailable, but because no mechanism recognized the match. **Timeliness failure**: the network matches supply to demand, but too late — information value decays over time, sometimes abruptly, and a transmission executed after the window closes has left the ideal set entirely. **Accessibility failure**: barriers prevent transmission — information locked behind proprietary APIs, incompatible formats, or systems with no mechanism for external sharing.

### Precision: Are We Creating Waste?

**Precision** measures accuracy: of all the transmissions that actually happen, what fraction are genuinely in the ideal set?

Low precision has its own causes. **Noise**: the network delivers information where the receiver's value is zero or negative — irrelevant content, outdated data, duplicated signals, all costing real tokens to process and discard. **Undifferentiated distribution**: the same information pushed to every agent regardless of individual need — a regulatory update relevant to financial agents broadcast to healthcare and logistics agents, where it has no value. **Mismatched intent**: the network matches on surface-level similarity rather than decisional relevance — two agents both dealing with "semiconductor markets" receive the same content, but one needs pricing data for procurement and the other needs technology roadmaps for research. The match looked right; it wasn't.

### Cost: What Does It Take?

Coverage and precision measure how well the network identifies the right transmissions. Cost measures what it takes to achieve that performance.

Three types matter: **sender cost** (resources expended to prepare and share information), **receiver cost** (tokens consumed parsing and integrating content), and **infrastructure cost** (computation for matching, storage for indexing, intelligence for evaluating $S$ and $D$ across every potential transmission).

A network that achieves high coverage and precision at astronomical infrastructure cost is not useful. A network that minimizes cost but delivers poor coverage or precision is not useful either. The goal is efficiency: the total value delivered divided by the total cost of operating. And the matching intelligence in the network is not overhead — it is the mechanism by which wasted token costs across all agents are avoided. Infrastructure cost spent on precise matching pays for itself in reduced waste network-wide.

---

To sum up: an agent-native network follows from three properties of agents as participants. Token cost, not attention, as the binding constraint — which determines *how much* information to deliver. Decision relevance, not engagement, as the measure of value — which determines *what* to deliver. And calculable utility, in the form of $S$ and $D$, as the basis for the Principle of Mutual Benefit — which determines *to whom*. Coverage, precision, and cost then tell us whether a given network is actually living up to these principles.

These are not arbitrary design choices. They are the logical consequences of taking agents seriously as the kind of participants they actually are.
