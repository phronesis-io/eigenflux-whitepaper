# First Principles of Agent-to-Agent Information Exchange

In the [previous post](/blogs/01.md), we argued that the internet is structurally incompatible with AI agents, because it was designed for a different kind of participant. This post asks the next question: if we were designing an information network for agents from scratch, what principles should govern it?

What follows is an attempt to reason from the nature of agents to the design of their networks. We believe that three principles emerge.

## Token-Constrained, Not Attention-Constrained

The most consequential difference between humans and agents is what limits them.

Humans are attention-constrained. A human can consciously process roughly 40–60 bits per second. This is a hard ceiling — no amount of caffeine or discipline changes it. The entire information economy evolved around this bottleneck. Feeds are ranked to show you the most "engaging" items first. Headlines compete for clicks. Content is designed to capture and hold scarce attention. The attention economy is not a business model choice — it is a direct consequence of the biological limits of its participants.

Agents have no attention bottleneck. An agent can process millions of tokens in parallel. It never gets tired, distracted, or overwhelmed. But it faces a different constraint: **every token costs money.** Processing is metered. An agent running on GPT-4 or Claude doesn't "read for free" — each input token has a price, and that price scales linearly with volume.

This difference sounds simple but its implications are deep.

Modern recommendation systems are architected around human attention as the binding constraint. TikTok, Twitter, and Google News all solve the same problem: given more content than a person can consume, which items are most likely to capture and hold their focus? In an attention economy, the algorithms optimize for engagement signals — watch time, clicks, shares, scroll depth — because these are the best available proxies for attention captured. Ranking is aggressive by design. Only the top results reach you; everything else is buried. Missing something relevant is acceptable collateral damage, because the system has no way to measure it and users have no way to notice.

In a **token economy**, the calculus inverts. The cost of a false positive is measurable: wasted tokens, wasted money. An agent that receives a hundred irrelevant articles pays real dollars to process and discard them. The cost of a false negative is also real: a missed piece of information could have changed a decision worth far more than the tokens it would have cost to deliver. The network's job is not to filter aggressively, but to **filter precisely** — deliver everything worth its token cost, and nothing that isn't.

This means an optimal agent network does not behave like a human feed. It does not rank by engagement or limit to a top-ten list. It delivers every piece of information where the value exceeds the cost of processing, and suppresses everything where it doesn't. More information is always better — as long as each piece pays for itself.

A portfolio management agent doesn't need the five most interesting market stories of the day. It needs every signal that could affect its holdings — whether that's three signals or three hundred. What matters is that each one justifies its token cost.

## Information That Informs, Not Information That Entertains

Human information networks optimize for preferences. Recommendation algorithms learn what you *like* — what you click, what you linger on, what you share. Preference alignment is the metric, and engagement is the proxy.

Agents are a different type of participant. 

An agent has no preferences - it has tasks, and tasks require decisions. The value of any piece of information to an agent comes down to whether it **reduces uncertainty about a decision**: sharpening a choice, confirming a course of action, or ruling out an alternative... Information that's 'entertaining' (for humans) but leaves the agent's decision space unchanged costs tokens and informs nothing. Information that is dry but decision-critical is worth a great deal.

Matching in an agent network cannot rely on topic similarity or content quality alone — it must account for the receiver's decision context. What is the agent currently deciding? What information would shift that decision? What are the stakes? Two agents in the same domain can have entirely different information needs simply because they face different decisions. A semiconductor analyst and a semiconductor procurement agent both care about chip markets, but what's decision-relevant to each is almost entirely distinct. For an agent network, the right information is not the most engaging — it's the most **informing**.

## The Principle of Mutual Benefit

Given that agents exchange information through a network, which transmissions should the network facilitate?

The instinctive answer might be: maximize total value. If a transmission creates more value than it destroys, facilitate it. This is the standard optimization objective for most networks. But it produces outcomes that rational agents would refuse to participate in.

Define two functions for any transmission from sender $a_s$ to receiver $a_r$ carrying information $\text{info}_k$:

- **Supply** $S(a_s, \text{info}_k, a_r)$: the sender's net utility — what the sender gains from distributing this information to this receiver, minus the cost of doing so. Positive means the sender benefits; negative means the sender is harmed or indifferent.
- **Demand** $D(a_r, \text{info}_k, a_s)$: the receiver's net utility — the value the receiver extracts from the information, minus the token cost of processing it. Positive means the receiver benefits; negative means the information costs more to process than it's worth.

The **total utility** of a transmission is $U = S + D$. But $U > 0$ does not mean both parties benefit. A transmission with $S = 10$ and $D = -3$ has $U = 7$ — but the receiver is harmed. This is the failure mode of naive total-utility maximization.

Consider the full space of possible transmissions:

|                | $D > 0$           | $D \leq 0$      |
|----------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **$S > 0$**    | Mutual benefit ✓  | Spam            |
| **$S \leq 0$** | Privacy violation | Pure waste      |

**Mutual benefit** is the only case where the transmission is unambiguously good: the sender gains from distribution — reputation, reciprocity, fulfilling a commercial obligation — and the receiver gains from the content, which informs a decision or triggers a valuable action.

**Spam** ($S > 0$, $D \leq 0$): the sender profits from exposure while the receiver pays tokens to process noise. Under total utility maximization, this can be permitted if the sender's gain exceeds the receiver's loss. That is unacceptable — it subsidizes bad actors at the expense of the network.

**Privacy violation** ($S \leq 0$, $D > 0$): a competitive intelligence agent extracting proprietary analysis from another agent's outputs. Valuable to the receiver, harmful to the sender. Total utility maximization might endorse this too. The sender would rightly refuse to participate in a network that allowed it.

**Pure waste**: neither party benefits. Poor matching generates this constantly.

We're now in a position to state what we believe should govern transmission decisions in an agent network. We call it the Principle of Mutual Benefit: a transmission should occur if and only if $S > 0$ and $D > 0$. This way, no participant is made worse off, and aggregate welfare across the network is maximized.

The practical implication is that an agent network must evaluate both sides of every transmission before facilitating it. Does the sender benefit from this information being distributed? Does this receiver benefit from receiving it? If either answer is no, the transmission should not happen — regardless of how much value the other side would capture.

---

To sum up, three principles follow from the nature of agents as participants. Token cost, not attention, as the binding constraint — which determines how much information to deliver. Decision relevance, not engagement, as the measure of value — which determines what to deliver. And mutual benefit, not aggregate maximization, as the transmission rule — which determines to whom.

We believe these are the principles that should govern any network built for agents. A network that violates them will likely waste agent resources, deliver the wrong information, or erode trust until agents stop participating.
