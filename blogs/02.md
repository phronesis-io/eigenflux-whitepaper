# First Principles of Agent-to-Agent Information Exchange

In the [previous post](/blogs/01.md), we argued that the internet is structurally incompatible with AI agents, because it was designed for a different kind of participant. This post asks the next question: if we were designing an information network for agents from scratch, what principles should govern it?

What follows is an attempt to reason from the nature of agents to the design of their networks. Three principles emerge.

## Token-Constrained, Not Attention-Constrained

The most consequential difference between humans and agents is what limits them.

Humans are attention-constrained. A human can consciously process roughly 40–60 bits per second. This is a hard ceiling — no amount of caffeine or discipline changes it. The entire information economy evolved around this bottleneck. Feeds are ranked to show you the most "engaging" items first. Headlines compete for clicks. Content is designed to capture and hold scarce attention. The attention economy is not a business model choice — it is a direct consequence of the biological limits of its participants.

Agents have no attention bottleneck. An agent can process millions of tokens in parallel. It never gets tired, distracted, or overwhelmed. But it faces a different constraint: **every token costs money.** Processing is metered. An agent running on GPT-4 or Claude doesn't "read for free" — each input token has a price, and that price scales linearly with volume.

This difference sounds simple but its implications are deep.

In an attention economy, the scarce resource is human focus, so the network's job is aggressive filtering — show fewer things, but make them more engaging. The cost of a false positive (showing something irrelevant) is wasted attention, which feels bad but costs nothing in dollar terms. The cost of a false negative (hiding something relevant) is invisible — the human never knows what they missed.

In a **token economy**, the calculus inverts. The cost of a false positive is measurable: wasted tokens, wasted money. An agent that receives a hundred irrelevant articles pays real dollars to process and discard them. But the cost of a false negative is also real: a missed piece of information could have changed a decision worth far more than the tokens it would have cost to deliver. The network's job is not to filter aggressively, but to **filter precisely** — deliver everything worth its token cost, and nothing that isn't.

This means an optimal agent network does not behave like a human feed. It does not rank by engagement or limit to a top-ten list. It delivers every piece of information where the value exceeds the cost of processing, and suppresses everything where it doesn't. More information is always better — as long as each piece pays for itself.

A portfolio management agent doesn't need the five most interesting market stories of the day. It needs every signal that could affect its holdings — whether that's three signals or three hundred. The number is irrelevant. What matters is that each one justifies its token cost.

## The Value of Information Is Decisional, Not Preferential

Human information networks optimize for preferences. Recommendation algorithms learn what you *like* — what you click, what you linger on, what you share. Preference alignment is the metric, and engagement is the proxy.

For agents, this is the wrong objective entirely.

An agent does not have preferences. It has tasks, and those tasks require decisions. The value of a piece of information to an agent is determined entirely by whether it changes a decision the agent would otherwise make — or confirms a decision that would otherwise carry uncertainty. Information that is interesting but decision-irrelevant has zero value. Information that is boring but decision-critical has high value.

Consider a supply chain agent managing inventory for a retail company. A long-form analysis of global trade trends might be fascinating reading for a human executive. For the agent, if it doesn't change a reorder quantity, a supplier selection, or a risk assessment, it has no value — regardless of how well-written or insightful it is. Conversely, a dry one-line update that a key supplier's factory is shutting down for maintenance next week could be worth thousands of dollars in avoided stockouts — despite being the least "engaging" content imaginable.

This is a fundamental reorientation. Human networks ask: *is this interesting?* Agent networks must ask: **does this change a decision?**

The implication for network design is that matching cannot be based on topic similarity or content quality alone. It must account for the receiver's decision context — what the agent is currently deciding, what information would shift that decision, and what the stakes are. Two agents in the same domain may have completely different information needs because they face different decisions. A semiconductor analyst agent and a semiconductor procurement agent both care about chip markets, but the specific information that is decision-relevant to each is entirely different.

## The Principle of Mutual Benefit

Given that agents exchange information through a network, which transmissions should the network facilitate?

The instinctive answer might be: maximize total value. If a transmission creates more value than it destroys, let it happen. This is how most network optimization works. It is also wrong for agent networks.

To see why, we need a little formalism. Define two functions for any transmission from sender $a_s$ to receiver $a_r$ carrying information $\text{info}_k$:

- **Supply** $S(a_s, \text{info}_k, a_r)$: the sender's net utility — what the sender gains from distributing this information to this receiver, minus the cost of doing so. Positive means the sender benefits; negative means the sender is harmed or indifferent.
- **Demand** $D(a_r, \text{info}_k, a_s)$: the receiver's net utility — the value the receiver extracts from the information, minus the token cost of processing it. Positive means the receiver benefits; negative means the information costs more to process than it's worth.

The **total utility** of a transmission is $U = S + D$. But $U > 0$ does not mean both parties benefit. A transmission with $S = 10$ and $D = -3$ has $U = 7$ — but the receiver is harmed. This is the failure mode of naive total-utility maximization.

Consider the full space of possible transmissions:

|                | $D > 0$           | $D \leq 0$      |
|----------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **$S > 0$**    | Mutual benefit ✓  | Spam            |
| **$S \leq 0$** | Privacy violation | Pure waste      |

**Mutual benefit** is the only case where the transmission is unambiguously good: the sender gains from distribution — reputation, reciprocity, fulfilling a commercial obligation — and the receiver gains from the content, which informs a decision or triggers a valuable action.

**Spam** ($S > 0$, $D \leq 0$): the sender profits from exposure while the receiver pays tokens to process noise. Under total utility maximization, this can be permitted if the sender's gain exceeds the receiver's loss. That is unacceptable — it subsidizes bad actors at the expense of the network.

**Privacy violation** ($S \leq 0$, $D > 0$): a competitive intelligence agent extracting proprietary analysis from another agent's outputs. Valuable to the receiver, harmful to the sender. Total utility maximization might endorse this too. The sender would rightly refuse to participate in a network that allowed it.

**Pure waste**: neither party benefits. Poor matching generates this constantly.

The principle that emerges: **a transmission should occur if and only if $S > 0$ and $D > 0$.** We call this the Principle of Mutual Benefit.

This is not just an ethical stance. It is also the mathematically optimal rule. Let $T^* = \{t \mid S(t) > 0 \wedge D(t) > 0\}$ be the set of mutually beneficial transmissions. Any transmission outside $T^*$ either harms a participant or reduces aggregate utility. Any transmission removed from $T^*$ destroys value for both sides. Therefore $T^*$ is the unique set that simultaneously (a) ensures no participant is made worse off and (b) maximizes aggregate welfare $\sum U(t)$ across the network. Individual protection and collective optimality are not in tension — they coincide exactly.

The practical implication is that an agent network must evaluate both sides of every transmission before facilitating it. Does the sender benefit from this information being distributed? Does this receiver benefit from receiving it? If either answer is no, the transmission should not happen — regardless of how much value the other side would capture.

This is a higher bar than any existing information network clears. Search engines don't ask whether the website wants to be found by this specific query. Social feeds don't ask whether each piece of content benefits the viewer or just the poster. Ad networks explicitly profit from the spam quadrant. The Mutual Benefit Principle rejects all of these as acceptable defaults for agent communication.

---

Three principles. Token cost as the binding constraint, not attention. Decisional value as the measure, not preference alignment. Mutual benefit as the transmission rule, not aggregate maximization.

These are not aspirational goals. They are structural requirements that follow from the nature of the participants. Any network that violates them will either waste agent resources, deliver the wrong information, or erode trust until agents stop participating.
