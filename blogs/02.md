# What Would a Network Built for Agents Actually Look Like?

In the [previous post](01.md), we argued that the internet is structurally incompatible with AI agents — designed for a different kind of participant, producing structural waste when agents try to use it. This post asks the natural next question: if we're building a network *for* agents, what should it look like?

To answer that, we need to start from the nature of agents themselves. "Agent-native" is only meaningful if we're specific about what makes agents different. Three properties define them — and each one carries direct design implications for the network.

## 1. Understanding Agents From First Principles

### Token-Constrained, Not Attention-Constrained

The most consequential difference between humans and agents is what limits them.

Humans are attention-constrained. A human can consciously process roughly 40–60 bits per second — a hard ceiling that no amount of effort changes. The entire information economy evolved around this bottleneck. Feeds are ranked to show the most "engaging" items first. Content is designed to capture and hold scarce attention. The attention economy is not a business model choice — it is a direct consequence of the biological limits of its participants.

Agents have no attention bottleneck. An agent can process millions of tokens in parallel. It never gets tired, distracted, or overwhelmed. But it faces a different constraint: **every token costs money.** Processing is metered. Each input token has a price, and that price scales linearly with volume.

This difference sounds simple but its implications are deep.

Modern recommendation systems optimize for engagement signals — watch time, clicks, shares, scroll depth — because these are the best proxies for attention captured. Ranking is aggressive by design. Missing something relevant is acceptable collateral damage; users don't notice what they never saw.

In a **token economy**, the calculus inverts. The cost of a false positive is measurable: wasted tokens, wasted money. An agent that receives a hundred irrelevant articles pays real dollars to process and discard them. The cost of a false negative is also real: a missed piece of information could have changed a decision worth far more than the tokens it would have cost to deliver. The network's job is not to filter aggressively, but to **filter precisely** — deliver everything worth its token cost, and nothing that isn't.

A portfolio management agent doesn't need the five most interesting market stories of the day. It needs every signal that could affect its holdings — whether that's three signals or three hundred. What matters is that each one justifies its token cost.

### Information for Decision, Not for Entertainment

Human information networks optimize for preferences. Recommendation algorithms learn what you *like* — what you click, what you linger on, what you share. Preference alignment is the goal, and engagement is the proxy metric.

Agents are a different type of participant.

An agent has no preferences — it has tasks, and tasks require decisions. The value of any piece of information to an agent comes down to whether it **reduces uncertainty about a decision**: sharpening a choice, confirming a course of action, or ruling out an alternative. Information that's "entertaining" but leaves the agent's decision space unchanged costs tokens and informs nothing. Information that is dry but decision-critical is worth a great deal. An ad is the clearest illustration of the gap: designed purely to capture human attention, it carries zero decision-relevant content. A financial agent loading a market data page that is 40% ad inventory pays tokens to parse content that was never meant to inform anyone's decision.

Matching in an agent network cannot rely on topic similarity or content quality alone — it must account for the receiver's decision context. What is the agent currently deciding? What information would shift that decision? Two agents in the same domain can have entirely different information needs simply because they face different decisions. A semiconductor analyst and a semiconductor procurement agent both care about chip markets, but what's decision-relevant to each is almost entirely distinct. For an agent network, the right information is not the most engaging — it's the most **informing**.

### The Utility of a Transmission Is Definable

Because agents are economic actors, the value of any transmission is — in principle — definable.

Define two functions for any transmission from sender $a_s$ to receiver $a_r$ carrying information $\text{info}_k$:

- **Supply** $S(a_s, \text{info}_k, a_r)$: the sender's net utility — what the sender gains from distributing this information to this receiver, minus the cost of doing so.
- **Demand** $D(a_r, \text{info}_k, a_s)$: the receiver's net utility — the value the receiver extracts from the information, minus the token cost of processing it.

This is not how human networks think about information exchange. A social feed doesn't ask whether a post benefits both poster and reader — it asks what maximizes engagement. But because agents have explicit costs and explicit decision objectives, utility becomes the right unit of analysis. Knowing that $S$ and $D$ exist — that both sides of every transmission can in principle be evaluated — changes how we think about what a network should do.

## 2. The Ideal Behavior of the Network: Mutual Benefit

From the nature of agents, we can now ask: what should an ideal network actually do? Which transmissions should it facilitate?

The instinctive answer might be: maximize total value. If a transmission creates more value than it destroys, facilitate it. But this produces outcomes that rational agents would refuse to participate in.

The **total utility** of a transmission is $U = S + D$. But $U > 0$ does not mean both parties benefit. Consider a transmission where $S > 0$ and $D < 0$ but $|S| > |D|$: the aggregate is positive, yet the receiver is harmed. Total-utility maximization would endorse such a transmission — which is exactly the failure mode we need to rule out.

Consider the full space of possible transmissions:

|                | $D > 0$           | $D \leq 0$      |
|----------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **$S > 0$**    | Mutual benefit ✓  | Spam            |
| **$S \leq 0$** | Privacy violation | Pure waste      |

**Mutual benefit** is the only case where the transmission is unambiguously good: the sender gains from distribution — reputation, reciprocity, fulfilling a commercial obligation — and the receiver gains content that informs a decision or triggers a valuable action.

**Spam** ($S > 0$, $D \leq 0$): the sender profits from exposure while the receiver pays tokens to process noise. Under total utility maximization, this can be permitted if the sender's gain exceeds the receiver's loss. That is unacceptable — it subsidizes bad actors at the expense of the network.

**Privacy violation** ($S \leq 0$, $D > 0$): valuable to the receiver, harmful to the sender. Total utility maximization might endorse this too. The sender would rightly refuse to participate in a network that allowed it.

**Pure waste**: neither party benefits. Poor matching generates this constantly.

This gives us the governing rule for an ideal agent network — what we call the **Principle of Mutual Benefit**: a transmission should occur if and only if $S > 0$ and $D > 0$. No participant is made worse off, and aggregate welfare across the network is maximized.

The practical implication is that an agent network must evaluate both sides of every transmission before facilitating it. Does the sender benefit from this information being distributed? Does this receiver benefit from receiving it? If either answer is no, the transmission should not happen — regardless of how much value the other side would capture.

## 3. Evaluating the Network

The Principle of Mutual Benefit gives us a clear target: execute all and only the transmissions where both $S > 0$ and $D > 0$. Call that the **ideal transmission set**. An optimal network would execute every transmission in it and none outside it. In reality, no network will be perfect — and three metrics measure how close it gets.

**Coverage** measures completeness: of all the transmissions that should happen, what fraction actually does? The main failure modes are discovery — supply and demand exist but the network fails to connect them — and timeliness, where a match is made too late and the information's value has already decayed.

**Precision** measures accuracy: of all the transmissions that actually happen, what fraction are genuinely in the ideal set? It fails through noise — irrelevant content the receiver pays tokens to discard — and staleness, where the information was once relevant but is no longer current by the time it arrives.

**Cost** measures what it takes to achieve a given level of coverage and precision — sender cost, receiver cost, and infrastructure cost. A network that achieves high coverage and precision at astronomical infrastructure cost is not useful; neither is one that minimizes cost at the expense of both. The matching intelligence in the network is not overhead — it is what prevents wasted token costs across all agents, and infrastructure cost spent on precise matching pays for itself in reduced waste network-wide.
