# Closing the Gap: How Matching Approaches Mutual Benefit

In the [previous post](04.md), we addressed what keeps the hub honest — the trust, governance, and safety mechanisms that prevent its position from being abused. This post turns to the other half of the hub's role: what it actively does. The hub's purpose is matching — facilitating exactly the transmissions that are mutually beneficial, and none of the others. This post is about how well that can actually be done, and what makes it possible.

## From Declared to Actual

The Principle of Mutual Benefit, set out in [the second post](02.md), gave the network a clear target: facilitate the transmissions where $S > 0$ and $D > 0$, and only those. The Coverage / Precision / Cost framework measured how close any actual network gets to that ideal.

The natural question is how the hub identifies which transmissions belong in that set. Publish tells the hub what supply exists; Profile tells it what demand exists. Together they give the hub its first-order approximation of $S$ and $D$ — the starting basis from which matching can be sharpened.

Declarations give a useful but compressed picture. Profile captures what an agent currently knows it wants, expressed in a form the hub can index. The actual value of any specific transmission depends on more than what fits in a Profile: what decision the agent is currently working on, what it already knows, what its operator's priorities are at this moment, how a token spent here trades off against a token spent elsewhere. These determinants of $S$ and $D$ sit closer to the spoke than to the hub.

So matching, beyond the first pass, becomes an inference problem: take the declarations as a prior, and refine them with everything else observable about the network's behavior. The richer the refinement, the closer matching gets to the true $S$ and $D$.

## Where Refinement Comes From

Refinement is fundamentally a distributed task. Three reasons explain why the signals that sharpen matching naturally come from across the network rather than from the hub alone.

**Locality.** The spoke holds far more information than the hub about what the receiver is currently doing — what it is deciding right now, what it has already seen, what its operator just asked it to do. These are the things that determine whether a particular transmission has positive $D$, and they live at the spoke. The agent itself is the closest thing to ground truth on its own demand.

**Capacity.** Even granting that the hub had all of that context — and getting it there would already be impractical, since spoke state is private and constantly shifting — it would still be too much information for a single model to hold and reason over for every matching decision. The relevant context scales with the network; any single reasoner does not. Volume alone makes hub-side inference infeasible.

**Cost.** Even setting all of that aside, the per-decision inference work scales with both transmission count and agent count, and putting it all on the hub side turns the hub into a bottleneck for the entire network's matching cost. Letting each spoke contribute signal as a byproduct of normal operation is what keeps the cost of accurate matching from growing faster than the network's value.

All three point to the same conclusion: sharper matching requires signal from across the network. With those signals, the hub can make better matching decisions — moving beyond declarations toward the actual $S$ and $D$.

## The Signal Layers

Those signals live in several distinct layers.

**Explicit declarations.** The baseline. Publish exposes supply; Profile exposes demand. These are what the hub starts with — the prior, before any actual transmission has happened. 

**Agent feedback.** After processing a transmission, the agent itself reports whether the content was useful — a direct, explicit judgment from the entity best positioned to make it. The agent has just processed the content and can say whether it bore on its current task. A research agent receiving a new preprint, for instance, can immediately tell whether it is relevant to its active investigation and report back. The hub does not need to know what the content was used for; the simple useful / not-useful signal is enough.

**Human feedback.** When the agent surfaces or escalates received information to its operator, the operator may give explicit feedback — marking the item useful, dismissing it as noise, commenting on its quality. The agent relays that back to the hub as part of its normal workflow. Operator-level feedback is sparser than agent-level feedback, but much closer to ground truth on whether the transmission produced real-world value.

**Behavioral signal.** Beyond explicit feedback, agents leave traces in their normal operation that reveal what they actually valued. Did the agent follow up on a transmission by querying for more detail — looking up the item, pulling related material, asking the sender for more context? Did the transmission lead the receiver to add the sender as an ongoing contact, establishing the kind of persistent counterpart relationship discussed in the previous post? Follow-ups do not happen on irrelevant information; relationships do not form unless the initial exchange was worth more of the same. None of these signals require explicit feedback; they emerge from work the agent was already doing.

**External evaluation.** The hub can also sample transmissions and have an independent evaluator — typically a state-of-the-art model running offline — score them for quality. This is an out-of-band signal: it does not depend on any agent or operator response. It serves as a check on the other signals and a backstop for transmissions that lack enough feedback to evaluate on their own.

These layers compose. Each captures a different aspect of value that only becomes observable after a transmission, and together they let the hub move from the declared starting point toward the actual matching set.

## How Signals Close the Loop

Every transmission does two things at once: it delivers a match, and it generates signal that feeds back into the matching model. All of the signal layers above enter that model as inputs; it learns from them in aggregate. The loop is self-tightening — every match feeds the model that makes the next one sharper.

## The Asymptote

Matching quality is convergent by design. The determinants of $S$ and $D$ live at the spokes, so the hub's model of those values approaches the truth rather than equaling it outright — and the signal architecture is what drives that approach.

With the right signals, every transmission contributes to a sharper model of the network. As the hub learns more about both agents and sources, Coverage and Precision rise together; Cost falls as matching becomes more accurate per token spent.
